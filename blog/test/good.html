△ How to Live Well

What Is Fundamentally Good and Bad

Before any rules, we must state what we believe is fundamentally valuable and what is fundamentally harmful. These are not proved — they are the bedrock on which everything rests. We state them openly so you can decide whether to accept them.

✓ Being alive is preferable to not being alive

The most basic value: conscious existence is generally preferable to non-existence. This is not absolute (suffering can make it debatable), but it is the default assumption without which no ethics makes sense.

✓ Reducing suffering is good

Where conscious beings exist, reducing their unnecessary suffering is fundamentally valuable. Not all suffering can be eliminated, but gratuitous suffering — suffering that serves no growth, no learning, no protection — is bad.

Exemplar — Harriet Tubman (c.1822–1913): Born into slavery, she escaped and then returned 13 times to lead approximately 70 enslaved people to freedom via the Underground Railroad. She risked her life repeatedly to reduce the suffering of others, saying "I never ran my train off the track and I never lost a passenger."

✓ Flourishing is preferable to mere survival

Beyond bare existence, the capacity to grow, create, love, and understand is valuable. A life of mere survival is better than death, but a life of flourishing is better still.

Exemplar — Frederick Douglass (1818–1895): After escaping slavery, he didn't merely survive — he became one of the greatest orators and writers of his century, proving that human flourishing, when unchained, knows no bounds of race or origin.

✓ Truth is preferable to delusion

Accurate understanding of reality, while sometimes painful, is fundamentally more valuable than comfortable falsehood. Delusion may feel good temporarily but leads to worse outcomes over time.

Exemplar — Ignaz Semmelweis (1818–1865): He discovered that handwashing prevented childbed fever, was ridiculed by the medical establishment, and died in an asylum. But his truth saved millions of lives after his death. Truth persists; delusion collapses.

✗ Gratuitous cruelty is bad

Causing suffering for its own sake — not in self-defense, not as an unavoidable side effect, but as a goal — is fundamentally wrong. This is perhaps the most universal moral intuition across all cultures.

Counter-example — Unit 731 (1937–1945): Japanese military scientists performed vivisection on living prisoners without anesthesia, purely for curiosity. No defensive purpose, no greater good — pure cruelty instrumentalized as "science." This is what bad looks like at its most undeniable.

✗ Dehumanization is bad

Treating conscious beings as objects, as less-than-persons, as means only — this is the root mechanism of nearly all large-scale evil. Every genocide begins with dehumanization.

Counter-example — The Rwandan genocide (1994): Radio Mille Collines called Tutsis "cockroaches" for months before the killing began. The linguistic dehumanization preceded and enabled the physical violence. 800,000 people were murdered in 100 days.

✗ Systematic deception is bad

Deliberately constructing false realities for others — gaslighting, propaganda, institutional lying — destroys the shared epistemic ground on which cooperation depends.

Counter-example — The tobacco industry (1950s–1990s): Internal documents showed they knew cigarettes caused cancer by 1953. They spent billions creating doubt, funding fake research, and lying to Congress. Estimated cost: 8 million preventable deaths.

These values are not proved. They are stated. If you do not share them, this system will not persuade you — but at least you can see clearly what it assumes and decide for yourself.
