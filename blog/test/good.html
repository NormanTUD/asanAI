What We Assume Is Good and Bad

Before any rules, we state our bedrock values openly. These are not proved — they are chosen. If you disagree with them, the system will not convince you, but at least you can see exactly where you diverge.

▾ Show the value foundations (optional)

✓ Being alive is preferable to not being alive

Conscious existence is generally preferable to non-existence. This is the default assumption without which no ethics makes sense.

Lived example — Janusz Korczak (1878–1942): A pediatrician who ran an orphanage in Warsaw. When the Nazis came to deport the children to Treblinka, he refused offers of escape and accompanied them so they would not die alone. He valued their lives — and their dignity in death — above his own survival.

✓ Reducing suffering is good

Where conscious beings exist, reducing their unnecessary suffering is fundamentally valuable. Not all suffering can be eliminated, but gratuitous suffering — suffering that serves no growth, no learning, no protection — is bad.

Lived example — Harriet Tubman (c.1822–1913): Born into slavery, she escaped and returned 13 times to lead approximately 70 enslaved people to freedom. She risked her life repeatedly to reduce the suffering of others.

✓ Flourishing is preferable to mere survival

Beyond bare existence, the capacity to grow, create, love, and understand is valuable. A life of mere survival is better than death, but a life of flourishing is better still.

Lived example — Frederick Douglass (1818–1895): After escaping slavery, he didn't merely survive — he became one of the greatest orators and writers of his century, proving that human flourishing, when unchained, knows no bounds.

✓ Truth is preferable to delusion

Accurate understanding of reality, while sometimes painful, is fundamentally more valuable than comfortable falsehood. Delusion may feel good temporarily but leads to worse outcomes over time.

Lived example — Ignaz Semmelweis (1818–1865): He discovered that handwashing prevented childbed fever, was ridiculed by the medical establishment, and died in an asylum. But his truth saved millions of lives after his death.

✗ Gratuitous cruelty is bad

Causing suffering for its own sake — not in self-defense, not as an unavoidable side effect, but as a goal — is fundamentally wrong. This is perhaps the most universal moral intuition across all cultures.

Counter-example — Unit 731 (1937–1945): Japanese military scientists performed vivisection on living prisoners without anesthesia, purely for curiosity. No defensive purpose, no greater good — pure cruelty instrumentalized as "science."

✗ Dehumanization is bad

Treating conscious beings as objects, as less-than-persons, as means only — this is the root mechanism of nearly all large-scale evil. Every genocide begins with dehumanization.

Counter-example — Rwanda (1994): Radio Mille Collines called Tutsis "cockroaches" for months before the killing began. The linguistic dehumanization preceded and enabled the physical violence. 800,000 people murdered in 100 days.

✗ Systematic deception is bad

Deliberately constructing false realities for others destroys the shared epistemic ground on which cooperation depends.

Counter-example — The tobacco industry (1950s–1990s): Internal documents showed they knew cigarettes caused cancer by 1953. They spent billions creating doubt. Estimated cost: 8 million preventable deaths.

These values are not proved. They are stated. If you do not share them, this system will not persuade you — but at least you can see clearly what it assumes and decide for yourself.

Connection to Other Axiomatic Systems

This ethical system does not exist in isolation. It connects to — and depends on — other axiomatic frameworks. Here we sketch how they relate.

Physical Axioms: The World Has Rules

Our system presupposes that the physical world is lawful — actions have predictable effects, causes precede effects, energy is conserved. Without physical regularity, the axiom "actions have effects" would be meaningless.

Connection: Our Axiom 4 (Actions Have Effects) is a special case of physical causality. If physics were random, ethics would be impossible — you could not predict the consequences of your choices. The reliability of physical law is what makes moral responsibility possible.

Logical Axioms: Consistency Is Possible

We presuppose classical logic (or at minimum, constructive logic for the Coq proofs): the law of non-contradiction, modus ponens, universal instantiation. Without logical consistency, no proof means anything.

Connection: Our sheaf-theoretic framework requires that local sections be consistent (the locality condition). This IS the logical axiom of non-contradiction applied to ethics: you cannot simultaneously hold contradictory moral positions on the same situation. The Coq formalization makes this explicit — the type-checker enforces logical consistency.

Mathematical Axioms: ZFC / Type Theory

The formal proofs use dependent type theory (Coq's Calculus of Inductive Constructions). This presupposes: types exist, functions exist, propositions-as-types, inductive definitions are well-founded.

Connection: Our "sheaf on a topological space" is a mathematical object defined within ZFC (or equivalently, within type theory via the univalence axiom). The gluing theorem is a standard result in sheaf theory — we apply it to ethics by interpreting open sets as domains of life (Self, Other, Community) and sections as ethical principles.

Biological Axioms: Agents Are Embodied

We presuppose that agents are finite, embodied, mortal beings with limited energy, attention, and time. This grounds Step 7 (Know Your Measure) — compactness is a biological fact before it is a mathematical theorem.

Connection: The compactness axiom (measure bounds length) is the formal expression of biological finitude. You cannot do infinitely many things because you have finite ATP, finite neurons, finite hours. This is not a limitation to overcome but a fact to respect. Every ethical system that ignores embodiment (demanding infinite compassion, infinite productivity, infinite vigilance) eventually breaks its adherents.

Social Axioms: Others Exist and Communicate

We presuppose intersubjectivity: other minds exist, communication is possible, shared meaning can be constructed. Without this, Steps 2 (See the Other), 3 (Tell the Truth), and 5 (Be Fair) are meaningless.

Connection: Our Axiom 3 (Others Exist) is the anti-solipsism axiom. It cannot be proved — solipsism is irrefutable. But it is the entry condition for all social ethics. If you accept it, the system works. If you reject it, you need no ethical system (but you also cannot read this document, since reading presupposes a communicating other who wrote it).

Evolutionary Axioms: Cooperation Is Stable

Game theory and evolutionary biology show that cooperation (tit-for-tat, reciprocal altruism) is an evolutionarily stable strategy under iterated interactions. Our ethical system is compatible with — and partially explained by — this fact.

Connection: Steps 0 (non-harm), 5 (fairness), and 6 (help) correspond to the cooperative strategies that dominate in iterated prisoner's dilemmas (Axelrod, 1984). This is not a JUSTIFICATION (the naturalistic fallacy), but it is an EXPLANATION of why these principles feel intuitive — they are the strategies that survived millions of years of social evolution.
▾ Formal sketch: how the systems connect (advanced)

The ethical system sits in a tower of dependencies. Each level presupposes the ones below it. You cannot have ethics without logic, logic without mathematics (in the formal sense), mathematics without set theory or type theory. But you also cannot have ethics without physics (causality), biology (embodiment), and sociology (intersubjectivity). The system is honest about these dependencies rather than pretending to be self-contained.
