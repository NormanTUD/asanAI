🌐 The Situs: How a Transformer Sees the World

An interactive visual guide to the Grothendieck situs conjecture — the idea that a language model's world-knowledge has the structure of a sheaf on a topological site, and that each layer of the transformer progressively resolves this structure from blurry global overview to sharp local detail.

1. The Metaphor: A Map You Can Zoom Into

Imagine you have a satellite photo of the entire Earth — but it's only 100×100 pixels. You can see continents, oceans, maybe the Sahara. But you can't read street signs or see individual trees.

Now imagine you have a magnifying lens you can place over any part of this blurry image. Where you place the lens, the image sharpens — you can see cities, then streets, then individual buildings. But the lens has a fixed size: the more you zoom in on one area, the less you see of everything else.

This is exactly what a transformer does with language. The embedding layer is the blurry satellite photo — everything is there, but compressed and superimposed. Each attention layer is a magnifying lens that chooses where to look. Each feed-forward layer sharpens the detail within the lens. And the final layer glues the sharpened patches back together into a coherent answer.

The mathematical framework that makes this precise is called a sheaf on a Grothendieck site — or, more poetically, a situs. Let's build up to it visually.

2. What Is a Situs? (The Landscape of Knowledge)

A situs (Grothendieck site) is a way of organizing knowledge into patches that overlap and cover each other. Think of it like a patchwork quilt where:

  • Each patch is a topic or concept (e.g., "mammals," "European capitals," "cooking verbs")
  • Patches can overlap (a "bat" belongs to both "mammals" and "flying things")
  • A set of patches covers a larger topic if together they exhaust it ("dogs" + "cats" + "horses" + … covers "domestic animals")

At each point on a patch, there's local information — a germ. All the germs at one point form a stalk. And a consistent assignment of data across a whole patch is a section.

3. The Embedding Layer: The Blurriest Photo

The embedding layer is the space of global sections — the coarsest, lowest-resolution image of the entire situs. Every concept the model has ever learned is compressed and superimposed into a fixed number of dimensions (typically 768 or more).

Think of it as a photograph where every image the camera has ever taken is printed on top of each other on the same piece of film. Everything is there — but it's all blurred together. This is polysemanticity: each dimension encodes many concepts at once.

Drag the superposition slider left to "de-blur" the embedding — watch concepts separate as if you're increasing the resolution. At full superposition (right), everything overlaps — this is what the model actually starts with.

4. Attention as a Magnifying Lens

Attention at each layer selects a covering: it decides which stalks to resolve and which germs to work with. This is the magnifying lens.

  • Narrow attention (low entropy) = sharp view of few stalks — like zooming in on one city
  • Broad attention (high entropy) = blurred view of many stalks — like seeing the whole continent

This is a resolution–breadth tradeoff enforced by the convex combination in fixed dimensionality. You can't zoom in everywhere at once.

Move the lens over the image. Narrow focus reveals fine detail in a small area. Broad focus sees more but blurs everything. This is the attention entropy tradeoff — you can't have both in fixed dimensions.

5. Feed-Forward: Expanding Local Geometry

Once attention has selected where to look, the feed-forward sub-layer locally expands the geometry within each selected patch. It deforms space so that information is encoded in the resulting Jacobian field, then compresses back into dimensionality d.

Imagine taking a rubber sheet (the embedding space) and stretching a small region of it — pulling apart the points so you can see fine details that were compressed together. Then you let the sheet snap back to its original size, but now the pattern of stretching carries the information.

6. Early Layers Route, Late Layers Resolve

The conjecture proposes a division of labor across the transformer's depth:

🔀 Early Layers: Routers

They tear open and rearrange the space to determine where on the situs to look. They connect words to words — figuring out syntactic structure, resolving references, grouping related tokens.

Like a librarian who, given a question, first figures out which section of the library to visit.

🔬 Late Layers: Resolvers

They connect concepts to concepts and work on ideas topologically, expanding local geometry at increasing resolution. They do the actual "thinking."

Like the librarian who, having found the right shelf, now reads and synthesizes the relevant passages.

7. Sheaf Gluing: Reassembling the World

The final layer performs sheaf gluing — reassembling locally resolved patches into a global section readable by the language-modeling head.

You've zoomed into different parts of the map, sharpened each one, and now you need to stitch them back together into a single coherent picture. The gluing condition says: where two patches overlap, they must agree. If they don't agree perfectly — because of polysemanticity and finite dimensions — you get approximate gluing, which is why the model sometimes contradicts itself.
Click Scatter Patches to begin the gluing process.

8. Hallucinations: Where the Map Lies

At the borders of expanded patches — where training coverage thins — the approximation degrades. The model confidently expands a region whose local structure was never properly learned, filling it with topologically plausible but factually wrong continuations.

Imagine your satellite map has high-resolution data for Europe and North America, but the data for central Africa is sparse. When you zoom into that region, the system invents plausible-looking terrain — rivers that don't exist, cities in the wrong place — because it extrapolates from patterns it learned elsewhere. It's not lying; it's filling in the blanks with its best geometric guess.

Move the query point into red zones (low coverage) to see how the model's confidence remains high even where its knowledge is thin — the geometric signature of hallucination.
