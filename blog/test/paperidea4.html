🌐 The Situs — How a Transformer Sees the World

1. The Core Idea — A Lens Drilling Through Layers

Imagine the model's knowledge as a stack of transparent layers. The bottom is the blurriest — everything superimposed. Each layer above sharpens certain regions. You hold a magnifying lens that drills through the stack. Where you point it, the layers combine to produce a sharp image. Move it elsewhere, and that region sharpens while the previous one blurs back.

This is the transformer pipeline. The embedding is the blurriest layer. Attention chooses where to point the lens. Feed-forward sharpens the detail. The final layer glues the sharpened patches into a readable answer.

Move mouse over canvas to position the lens. Slide Layer Depth to watch routing → resolving → gluing. Switch objects to see different topological structures.

2. The Holographic Embedding — Every Piece Contains the Whole

The embedding layer is the space of global sections — the coarsest image of the situs. Like a hologram, every piece contains the whole image, just at lower resolution.

Scratch half a photograph and half the image is gone forever. Scratch half a hologram and the entire image survives — just blurrier. The embedding works the same way: each dimension contributes to every concept.

3. Attention Heads as Lenses

Attention at each layer selects a covering: which stalks to resolve. Each attention head is a separate lens. Narrow = sharp view of few stalks. Broad = blurred view of many. A resolution–breadth tradeoff in fixed dimensions.

Drag the colored lens circles to reposition attention heads. Each head sharpens its region; the combined image (bottom-right) shows the final result. More heads = more coverage but each is weaker.

4. Feed-Forward — Expanding Local Geometry

The feed-forward sub-layer locally expands geometry within each selected patch — stretching space so information is encoded in the Jacobian field, then compressing back.

Take a rubber sheet and stretch a small region — pulling apart compressed points to see fine details. Then let it snap back. The pattern of stretching carries the information.

6. Hallucinations — Where the Map Lies

At patch borders where training thins, the model confidently expands a region never properly learned — filling it with topologically plausible but factually wrong continuations.

Your satellite map has great data for Europe but sparse data for central Africa. Zoom in there and the system invents plausible terrain — rivers that don't exist. It's filling blanks with its best geometric guess.

Move mouse into red zones — confidence stays high even where knowledge is thin.
