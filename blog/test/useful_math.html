The Hidden Algebra of Everything

Six structures that are the same structure, wearing different masks

∗ Convolution — Smearing as Algebra

Every measurement you've ever made is a convolution. Your eyes convolve photons with receptor response curves. Your memory convolves events with an exponential decay kernel. A blurry photo is the scene convolved with the lens's point spread function. Undoing convolution is deconvolution — and that's what sharpening, echo cancellation, and sobriety are.

The convolution theorem says: convolution in one domain is multiplication in the dual domain. This is why Fourier transforms exist — they turn the hard thing into the easy thing.

⟲ Fixed Points — The Inevitability of Equilibrium

If you crumple a map of a city and place it on top of an uncrumpled copy, exactly one point on the crumpled map sits directly above its corresponding point on the flat map. Always. This is Brouwer's theorem. It's why every continuous economic dynamic has an equilibrium (Nash), why every contraction has a unique solution (Banach), why iterating any reasonable function enough times lands you somewhere stable.

You find fixed points constantly: adjusting the shower temperature, negotiating a price, tuning a guitar string. You're iterating a map until f(x) = x.

𝐻 Entropy — Surprise is a Measure

Entropy is not disorder. It's how surprised you'd be on average. A fair coin: maximum surprise per bit. A loaded coin: less surprise, less information per flip. This is why compression works (remove redundancy = remove non-surprise), why learning feels like something (you update from high-entropy priors to low-entropy posteriors), and why hot things cool down (the universe maximizes total surprise).

KL-divergence measures the cost of being wrong about a distribution. Every time you're "caught off guard," you're paying KL-divergence between your model and reality.

𝓕 Duality — Every Signal Has a Secret Twin

The Fourier transform isn't about sound. It's about the fact that every space has a dual space where the hard problems become easy. Position ↔ momentum. Time ↔ frequency. Space ↔ spatial frequency. Convolution ↔ multiplication. Differentiation ↔ multiplication by iω. The transform is its own inverse (up to conjugation). This is the deepest symmetry in analysis.

When you "hear" a chord, your cochlea is computing a Fourier transform in hardware. When you see a pattern repeat, you're detecting a peak in spatial frequency space.

𝕄 The Manifold Hypothesis — High Dimensions Are Mostly Empty

A face is ~100,000 pixels, but the space of all actual faces is maybe 50-dimensional. This is the manifold hypothesis: real data lives on thin curved sheets in high-dimensional space. It's why PCA works, why neural nets generalize, why you can describe a person's face in a sentence instead of listing pixel values. The "curse of dimensionality" is really a blessing — because nothing real is actually high-dimensional.

Your brain does manifold learning constantly. You perceive a "room" as a low-dimensional concept despite receiving millions of photons. Abstraction IS dimensionality reduction.

G Group Actions — Symmetry is the Only Thing That's Conserved

Noether's theorem: every symmetry gives a conservation law. Time-translation symmetry → energy conservation. Rotation symmetry → angular momentum. Gauge symmetry → charge conservation. But also: every time you say "it doesn't matter which way I hold this," you're identifying a symmetry group. Every time you say "these are essentially the same," you're quotienting by a group action.

Classification IS finding the right group to quotient by. "Dog" as a concept is the orbit of all dog-images under the group of pose/lighting/breed transformations.

⟨ ∗, ⟲, 𝐻, 𝓕, 𝕄, G ⟩ — They're All the Same Thing

Convolution is a group action (translation group acting on functions). The Fourier transform decomposes functions into irreducible representations of that group. Fixed points are where the group action is trivial. Entropy measures how uniformly the group orbits are distributed. Manifolds are the quotient spaces of high-dimensional groups. It's all representation theory.
