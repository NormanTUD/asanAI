How a Transformer Learns Modular Addition

An interactive deep-dive into the Fourier Multiplication Algorithm
Based on Nanda et al. (ICLR 2023): "Progress Measures for Grokking via Mechanistic Interpretability"

Overview

A small 1-layer transformer is trained to compute (a + b) mod 113. After "grokking" (suddenly generalizing after long overfitting), the network implements a beautiful algorithm:

Embed a, b
as sin/cos
→
Attention
bilinear mixing
→
MLP (FFN)
trig identities
→
Unembed
constructive interference
→
Softmax
pick the peak

The key idea: the network converts addition into rotation on a circle, uses trigonometric identities to compose rotations, then uses constructive interference of cosine waves to identify the correct answer.

Step 1: Embedding onto the Circle

Step 1

The embedding matrix WE maps each number a (from 0 to 112) to points on circles at specific frequencies. For a frequency k, the number a maps to the angle wk · a where wk = 2πk / 113.

Key Insight: These are REAL sines and cosines — not vague approximations. The embedding matrix, after training, becomes sparse in the Fourier basis. Its columns are almost exactly sin(wk · a) and cos(wk · a) for 5 specific "key frequencies" k ∈ {14, 35, 41, 42, 52}. The fraction of variance explained is >93% for each.
Numbers 0-112 mapped onto a circle at frequency k. Each number becomes an angle.
a = 7 angle(a) = wk·a = 0.00 rad cos(wk·a) = 0.00 sin(wk·a) = 0.00

Why does the network learn sines/cosines? Because modular addition is inherently periodic with period P=113. The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) is the natural basis for periodic functions. The network discovers this through gradient descent + weight decay, which pushes it toward the simplest generalizing solution.

Are they "real" sines? Yes! The embedding matrix WE, when you take its Fourier transform along the input dimension, is extremely sparse — only 5-6 frequencies have significant energy. The paper shows >93% of variance is explained by exact trigonometric functions. Weight decay during training pushes the network to be clean and sparse in this basis.

Step 2: What Attention Does (Bilinear Mixing)

Step 2

The attention layer's job is subtle but crucial. It creates products of the sine/cosine embeddings of a and b. Here's why that matters:

To compute cos(wk(a+b)), you need the trig identity:

cos(wk(a+b)) = cos(wka)cos(wkb) - sin(wka)sin(wkb)

This requires MULTIPLYING cos(wka) with cos(wkb), etc.

But wait — transformers are mostly linear! How do you multiply? The attention mechanism is bilinear: it computes (attention weights) × (values). The attention weights depend on one input, and the values depend on the other. Their product gives you the cross-terms you need.

How attention creates products:

Attention score from '=' to 'a' for Head 0 (periodic in frequency k=35)
The mechanism in detail:
  1. The attention score from '=' to position 'a' is a lookup: Cj[a] ≈ F·cos(wka) + G·sin(wka)
  2. Since softmax over 2 elements is a sigmoid, and the sigmoid operates in a near-linear regime: attn(a) ≈ 0.5 + α(cos(wka) - cos(wkb))
  3. The OV circuit outputs values proportional to sin/cos of the same frequency
  4. Multiplying attention weights (function of a,b) by values (function of a or b) gives degree-2 products like cos(wka)·cos(wkb)

Why is softmax approximately linear here? The inputs to the sigmoid are in the range [-5, 5], where a linear approximation explains 97.5% of variance. Replacing sigmoid with its best linear fit actually improves model performance! So the softmax is NOT doing argmax here — it's acting as a smooth linear gate.

What about the other 2 heads? Heads 1 and 3 have attention patterns that nearly sum to 1 (they're opposites). Their OV circuits contain all 5 key frequencies. Their role is simply to amplify the embedding magnitudes in the residual stream — compensating for weight decay making WE small.

Step 3: The MLP (Feed-Forward Network) Computes Trig Identities

Step 3

After attention creates degree-2 products of sines and cosines, the MLP layer (with ReLU activation) refines these into clean cos(wk(a+b)) and sin(wk(a+b)) signals.

MLP neuron activations: each neuron computes a degree-2 polynomial of a single frequency
Each neuron i computes approximately:

neuron_i(a,b) ≈ ReLU(α·cos(wka)cos(wkb) + β·sin(wka)sin(wkb) + γ·cos(wka)sin(wkb) + ...)

84.6% of neurons (433/512) have >85% variance explained by a degree-2 polynomial of a SINGLE frequency.

How ReLU helps:

ReLU(x) = max(0, x) is the only nonlinearity in the MLP. While it doesn't directly compute trig identities, it allows the network to:

  • Combine the degree-2 terms from attention into the right linear combinations
  • The input to each neuron (from Win) selects and weights the products
  • ReLU clips negative values, but since many neurons work together per frequency, the full signal is reconstructed
  • The output weights (Wout) then project these into directions that WL can read
Critical finding: When you project MLP activations onto the directions uk and vk (defined by the unembedding), you get:
ukT · MLP(a,b) ≈ 44.1 · cos(wk(a+b))
vkT · MLP(a,b) ≈ 44.1 · sin(wk(a+b))
with >93% variance explained. The trig identity IS being computed!

Neurons are frequency-localized: The 512 neurons partition into clusters, each dedicated to one frequency. The 44 neurons in the k=14 cluster only connect to the sin(w14) and cos(w14) components of the output. This means computation is modular — each frequency is processed independently.

Step 4: Constructive Interference (How the Answer Emerges)

Step 4

This is the most beautiful part. The unembedding matrix WL = WUWout reads off the MLP activations and produces logits for each possible answer c ∈ {0, 1, ..., 112}.

Logit(c) ≈ Σk αk · cos(wk(a + b - c))

This is maximized when a + b - c ≡ 0 (mod 113), i.e., when c = (a+b) mod 113!
Logits for each possible answer c. The peak is at c = (a+b) mod 113.
Correct answer: (a+b) mod 113 = 72 Peak location: c = 72

Why multiple frequencies?

A single cosine cos(wk(a+b-c)) has its maximum at c = (a+b) mod 113, but it also has values very close to 1 at other points. For example, cos(2π·14/113 · 8) = 0.998 — almost indistinguishable from the true peak!

By adding 5 different frequencies together, the cosines constructively interfere at c* = (a+b) mod 113 (where ALL of them equal 1) and destructively interfere everywhere else (where they point in random directions and cancel out).

Individual cosine waves (colored) and their sum (white). Only at the correct answer do all waves align.

Step 5: Softmax Converts Logits to a Probability

Step 5

After the constructive interference creates a sharp peak in the logits at the correct answer, softmax converts these logits into a probability distribution.

softmax(logitc) = exp(logitc) / Σj exp(logitj)

Since the peak logit is MUCH larger than the rest, softmax concentrates
nearly all probability mass on the correct answer.
Softmax output: probability distribution over answers. Nearly all mass on correct answer.
Softmax's role is simple here: It's just converting the sharp peak (created by constructive interference) into a probability. The "intelligence" is NOT in the softmax — it's in the interference pattern that creates the peak. Softmax is just the final readout that says "the answer with the highest logit wins."

The output softmax vs. the attention softmax: Don't confuse these! The attention softmax (Step 2) acts as a near-linear gate for mixing. The output softmax (this step) acts as a near-argmax for selecting the answer. They play completely different roles.

Summary: The Complete Algorithm

Summary
INPUT: a, b ∈ {0, ..., 112}
GOAL: compute c = (a + b) mod 113

1. EMBED: WE maps a → [cos(wka), sin(wka)] for k ∈ {14, 35, 41, 42, 52}
(These are REAL sines, >93% variance explained)

2. ATTENTION: Creates products cos(wka)·cos(wkb), sin(wka)·sin(wkb), etc.
(Bilinear: attn_weights × values. Softmax is ~linear here.)

3. MLP: Combines products into cos(wk(a+b)) and sin(wk(a+b))
(Using trig identities. 84.6% of neurons dedicated to single frequency.)

4. UNEMBED: WL computes Logit(c) = Σk αk cos(wk(a+b-c))
(Constructive interference: all cosines = 1 only when c = (a+b) mod 113)

5. OUTPUT SOFTMAX: Converts sharp peak into probability ≈ 1 at correct answer.
