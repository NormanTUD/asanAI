1. The Big Idea: What is Higher Algebra?

Ordinary algebra studies structures like numbers, polynomials, and matrices where you can add and multiply things. The rules (like a·b = b·a for commutativity) hold exactly.

Higher algebra studies the same kinds of structures, but in a world where equations only hold up to a continuous deformation (a "homotopy"). Instead of saying a·b = b·a, we say "there exists a path from a·b to b·a" — and these paths themselves must be compatible in coherent ways.
Think of tying knots. In ordinary algebra, two expressions are either equal or not. In higher algebra, two expressions are "the same" if you can continuously deform one into the other — like untangling a rope without cutting it. The record of HOW you deformed it matters too!

2. Monoids: The Simplest Algebraic Structure

A monoid is a set with a binary operation that is associative and has a unit element. Examples: (ℕ, +, 0) or (ℕ, ×, 1).

A commutative monoid additionally satisfies a·b = b·a.
Click a button above to see the property visualized.
A ring (like the integers ℤ) is just a monoid for multiplication that lives inside the world of abelian groups (which handles the addition). This is the key insight: a ring = monoid in (Ab, ⊗).

3. Monoidal Categories: Where Monoids Live

A monoidal category is a category (a collection of objects and arrows between them) equipped with a "tensor product" ⊗ that lets you combine objects, plus a unit object 𝟙. The key: associativity and unitality hold only up to isomorphism, not equality — but these isomorphisms must satisfy "coherence conditions" (pentagon and triangle identities).

Objects: sets. Tensor: A×B. Unit: {*}. A monoid here = ordinary monoid.

Objects: abelian groups. Tensor: A⊗B. Unit: ℤ. A monoid here = ring.

Objects: pointed spaces. Tensor: X∧Y. Unit: S⁰. A monoid here = ring up to homotopy.

Objects: spectra. Tensor: X∧Y. Unit: 𝕊. A monoid here = ring spectrum.

4. Spectra: Stabilized Spaces

A spectrum is a sequence of pointed topological spaces (X₀, X₁, X₂, ...) connected by "structure maps" σₙ: S¹∧Xₙ → Xₙ₊₁. Think of it as a space that has been "stabilized" — all the unstable phenomena have been washed away, leaving only the stable homotopy information.

Spectra are to stable homotopy theory what abelian groups are to homological algebra.
Imagine a movie (sequence of frames). Each frame Xₙ is a topological space. The structure map is like a "zoom in" operation — it takes the previous frame, wraps it with a circle (S¹∧−), and maps it into the next frame. A spectrum is the entire movie, capturing information that stabilizes as you keep zooming.

5. Smash Product of Spectra: Multiplication for Spectra

The smash product X∧Y of two spectra is the "tensor product" in the world of spectra. It's the operation that lets you do algebra with spectra. Making this product strictly associative and commutative (not just up to homotopy) was a major achievement — it required moving from plain sequential spectra to "highly structured" spectra (symmetric spectra, orthogonal spectra, or S-modules).

The key technique is Day convolution: if you have a monoidal category 𝒞, then functors from 𝒞 to spaces inherit a tensor product by "convolving" over 𝒞.
Day Convolution Formula:
(X ⊗Day Y)(c) = ∫c₁,c₂ 𝒞(c₁⊗c₂, c) ∧ X(c₁) ∧ Y(c₂)

This "integral" (coend) sums over all ways to decompose c into a tensor product c₁⊗c₂, weighted by the values of X and Y.

6. E∞-Rings: Commutative Rings Up to All Higher Homotopies

An E∞-ring is a commutative monoid in the (∞,1)-category of spectra. It's the homotopy-theoretic generalization of a commutative ring.

"E∞" stands for "everything commutes, up to all coherent higher homotopies." The "E" refers to the little cubes operad En and ∞ means we take n→∞.

Concretely: in a model with symmetric monoidal smash product, an E∞-ring is just an ordinary commutative monoid object — the model category machinery handles all the homotopy coherence for free!

A∞-ring (E₁)

Associative up to coherent homotopies. Like a non-commutative ring, but flexible.

E₂-ring

Commutative up to homotopy, with one level of coherence for the commutativity homotopies.

E∞-ring

Commutative up to ALL higher coherent homotopies. The "fully commutative" case.

7. Model Categories: Doing Homotopy Theory Concretely

A model category is a category equipped with three classes of morphisms — weak equivalences (W), fibrations (Fib), and cofibrations (Cof) — satisfying axioms that let you do homotopy theory concretely. They form the "workshop" where you can compute with homotopical objects using 1-categorical tools.

The key axiom: (Cof, Fib∩W) and (Cof∩W, Fib) are weak factorization systems — meaning any morphism can be factored through them, and they satisfy lifting properties.
Think of a model category as a "workshop manual" for building things up to deformation. Cofibrations are like building up (adding cells), fibrations are like projecting down (nice surjections), and weak equivalences are the "same up to wiggling" maps. The factorization axiom says: any construction can be broken into a "build up" step followed by a "project down" step.

8. Coherence: Why Higher Homotopies Must Agree

When associativity holds only up to homotopy, you get an "associator" — a path from (a·b)·c to a·(b·c). But then for four elements, there are multiple paths from ((a·b)·c)·d to a·(b·(c·d)), and these must all be connected by a 2-homotopy. This is the pentagon identity.

Coherence means: the space of all possible ways to re-bracket (or re-order) is contractible — there's essentially only one way, even though it looks like there are many choices.
Imagine you're in a room with many doors leading to the same destination. Coherence says: not only can you reach the destination through any door, but the room itself can be shrunk to a point — all paths through it are equivalent. There's no "interesting topology" in the space of choices.
