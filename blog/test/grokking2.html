What They Actually Experimented With

Power et al. (2021) ran a systematic set of experiments on a tiny transformer learning binary operation tables. Here's exactly what they tried, what they varied, and what they found — interactively.

🔧 The Experimental Setup

What they built: A decoder-only transformer with 2 layers, 128 width, 4 attention heads (~400K non-embedding params). Input: 5 tokens ⟨a⟩ ⟨op⟩ ⟨b⟩ ⟨=⟩ ⟨c⟩. Loss computed only on the answer token c. Each element is an abstract symbol — no number structure given. Trained with AdamW, lr=10⁻³, weight decay=1, β₁=0.9, β₂=0.98, batch size 512, budget 10⁵ steps.

// Key: elements are ABSTRACT SYMBOLS
// "3" and "7" are as unrelated as "🍎" and "🚗"
// The network must discover structure from interactions alone

// Default optimizer
AdamW | lr=1e-3 | wd=1.0 | β=(0.9, 0.98)
Warmup: 10 steps | Batch: min(512, |train|/2)
Budget: 10⁵ gradient updates (10⁶ for Fig.1 demo)
Why this matters: The model is massively overparameterized for these tasks (400K params to learn a table with at most 97²=9409 entries). Classical theory says it should just memorize. The question: can it generalize anyway, and under what conditions?

📊 Experiment 1: Which Operations Generalize?

What they did: Tested 12 different binary operations. For each, swept training data fraction from 10% to 95%. Measured best validation accuracy after 10⁵ steps. 3 seeds each. The question: which algebraic structures can the network learn to generalize on?
50%
Key findings: (1) Symmetric operations (x+y, x²+y²) need less data than asymmetric ones (x-y, x/y). (2) x+y mod(p-1) and x*y mod p are mathematically equivalent (via primitive roots) — and indeed show identical curves. (3) Some operations (x³+xy²+y) NEVER generalize even at 95% data. (4) Mixed operations (div if odd, sub if even) CAN generalize — the network learns to dispatch.

⚗️ Experiment 2: The Ablation Study (S₅ Group Product)

What they did: Fixed the task to S₅ group multiplication (120 elements, 14400 possible equations). Tested 9 different optimizer/regularization configurations. Swept data fraction. Budget: 10⁵ steps. The question: what makes generalization happen?
Key findings: (1) Weight decay is king — halves the data needed. (2) Weight decay toward origin > toward initialization (so it's not just about staying near init). (3) Any noise helps (minibatch SGD, gradient noise, weight noise) — consistent with "noise finds flatter minima." (4) Learning rate must be within ~1 order of magnitude. (5) ALL methods reach 100% train acc in 10³-10⁴ steps regardless — generalization is the bottleneck, not optimization.

⏱️ Experiment 3: How Long Until Generalization?

What they did: For S₅ product, measured the median number of optimization steps until validation accuracy first hits 99%. Swept data fraction. Used 7 seeds. Budget increased to 5×10⁵. The question: how does compute trade off against data?
28%
14
Key findings: (1) Near 25-30% data, removing 1% of data → 40-50% MORE optimization steps needed. Exponential blowup! (2) Training time to 99% train acc actually DECREASES with less data (fewer examples = easier to memorize). (3) This is a genuine compute-for-data tradeoff: you can compensate for less data with more compute, up to a point.

🎭 Experiment 4: The Dramatic Demo (Division mod 97)

What they did: Trained on x/y (mod 97) with 50% data, Adam (NO weight decay), budget 10⁶ steps. This is the "hero figure" — maximum drama. The question: how extreme can the gap between memorization and generalization get?
50%
0.00 (no WD — max drama)
Key findings: (1) Train acc → 100% at ~10³ steps. (2) Val acc stays at CHANCE (1/97 ≈ 1%) until ~10⁵ steps. (3) Then suddenly shoots to 100% by ~10⁶ steps. (4) The validation LOSS actually INCREASES during memorization (double descent), peaking around 10⁵ before the second descent. (5) No weight decay was used here — to show grokking happens even without explicit regularization, just from SGD noise over long training.

🧪 Experiment 5: Can It Generalize Despite Wrong Labels?

What they did: Took a binary op dataset, randomly replaced k training labels with wrong answers (k ∈ {0, 10, 100, 1000, 2000, 3000}). Trained as usual. The question: does the network denoise, or does memorizing outliers prevent generalization?
0
Key findings: (1) The network ALWAYS reaches 100% train acc — it memorizes the wrong labels too. (2) Up to k=1000 outliers, generalization is barely affected! (3) At k=2000-3000, generalization degrades significantly. (4) This means: the network has capacity far beyond what's needed for memorization, and generalization requires a "non-trivial explanation" — it's not just about capacity constraints forcing the network to learn the pattern.

🔮 Experiment 6: What Does the Network Actually Learn Inside?

What they did: After training to generalization, extracted the output layer weight matrix. Applied t-SNE. For modular addition: found circular topology. For S₅: found coset clusters. The question: does the network discover the mathematical structure?
31
100% (fully grokked)
8
Key findings: (1) Modular addition → embeddings form a CIRCLE (the network discovered ℤ/pℤ is cyclic!). (2) Adding 8 to each element traces a path around the circle. (3) Colors by residue mod 8 cluster together — the network idiosyncratically organizes by subgroup structure. (4) S₅ → clusters correspond to cosets of specific subgroups. (5) Weight decay makes structure MORE apparent. (6) Slide "training progress" to 0% to see: before grokking, it's just noise.

🔗 Experiment 7: The Hidden Equivalence

What they did: Noticed that x+y (mod p-1) and x*y (mod p) are isomorphic (via discrete logarithm / primitive roots). Similarly x-y (mod p-1) ≅ x/y (mod p). Checked if the data efficiency curves match. They do!
97
Key findings: Since every nonzero residue mod p can be written as gᵏ for a primitive root g, multiplication mod p is just addition of exponents mod (p-1). The network doesn't "know" this — but since elements are abstract symbols, the two tasks are literally identical up to relabeling. This is a beautiful sanity check: the curves match perfectly, confirming the network treats symbols as truly abstract.
