What is a Manifold?

A manifold is a shape that locally looks like flat space — even if globally it's curved or twisted. Zoom into any small patch, and it looks like ordinary flat paper.

🖱️ Move your mouse over the surface — notice how every small patch looks flat

Manifold Examples

Every smooth surface you know is a manifold. A sphere, a torus (donut), even a flat plane. Click each to see it in action.

Circle (1D Manifold)

A circle is a 1D manifold: zoom into any point and it looks like a straight line.

Sphere (2D Manifold)

Earth's surface is a 2D manifold: locally flat (that's why old maps work!), globally curved.

Torus (2D Manifold)

A donut surface is also a manifold — every patch is locally flat, but the topology is different from a sphere.

Building Manifolds by Gluing

You can build manifolds by taking flat pieces and gluing edges together. A flat square with opposite edges identified becomes a torus!

Now... What is an Orbifold?

An orbifold is like a manifold, but it's allowed to have special singular points — places where the space has a symmetry "defect." Think of it as a manifold that's been folded by a symmetry.

Key Insight: Take a manifold and fold it using a symmetry (like folding a piece of paper). The fold lines and corners create "singular points" — the resulting shape is an orbifold.

Cone Points: The Heart of Orbifolds

When you fold a manifold around a point with n-fold symmetry, that point becomes a cone point. Around a cone point of order n, there's only 360°/n of angle instead of the full 360°.

A cone point of order n means only 1/n of the full neighborhood exists

A Classic Orbifold: The Teardrop

Take a sphere and add one cone point. You get a "teardrop" orbifold — smooth everywhere except at one special point. Or fold a sphere in half to get a "pillow" orbifold with corner points.

Summary

Manifold: A space that looks like flat ℝⁿ everywhere you zoom in. Smooth, no special points. Examples: sphere, torus, the surface of any smooth object.

Orbifold: A space that is almost a manifold, but has isolated singular points where the space has been "folded" by a symmetry. These cone points, mirror edges, and corner reflectors make orbifolds the natural language for describing symmetry in geometry.

Orbifolds generalize manifolds the same way rational numbers generalize integers — by allowing "fractional" local structure at special points.
