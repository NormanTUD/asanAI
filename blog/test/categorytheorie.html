1. Objects & Arrows

Forget what things are. A category has objects (dots) and morphisms (arrows between dots). That's it. The objects could be numbers, types, sets, people, ideas — it doesn't matter. All that matters is what connects to what.

🎮 Play: Build a Category

Key insight: In category theory, objects have no internal structure. An object is entirely defined by its arrows. You are your relationships.

2. Composition — The Only Rule

If there's an arrow f: A→B and an arrow g: B→C, there must be an arrow g∘f: A→C. That's composition. And every object must have an identity arrow id: A→A that does nothing. Composition must be associative: h∘(g∘f) = (h∘g)∘f.

🎮 Play: Composition Machine

Click two composable arrows to see their composition appear automatically.

Why it matters: Composition is why modular code works, why functions chain, why pipelines exist. Anything that composes reliably is categorical. f.then(g).then(h) — that's associativity.

3. Isomorphism — "Same" Has a Meaning

Two objects A and B are isomorphic if there exist arrows f: A→B and g: B→A such that g∘f = id_A and f∘g = id_B. They're "the same" in every categorical sense — you can go back and forth without losing anything.

🎮 Play: Isomorphism Detector

Two graphs. Drag nodes to rearrange. The system detects if they're isomorphic and shows the mapping.

Life lesson: A list and a linked list are isomorphic — different representations, same structure. Stop confusing the representation with the thing. Category theory teaches you to see through notation to structure.

4. Functors — Structure-Preserving Maps

A functor F: C→D maps objects to objects and arrows to arrows, preserving composition: F(g∘f) = F(g)∘F(f) and F(id) = id. It's a perfect analogy between two worlds.

🎮 Play: Functor Machine

✓ Valid functor
You already use functors: Array.map(f) is a functor. It takes a function between elements and lifts it to a function between arrays, preserving composition. [1,2,3].map(f).map(g) equals [1,2,3].map(x => g(f(x))). That's the functor law.

5. Natural Transformations — Morphisms Between Functors

If functors are analogies between worlds, a natural transformation is a way to compare two analogies. Given functors F,G: C→D, a natural transformation α: F⇒G assigns to each object A an arrow α_A: F(A)→G(A) such that everything commutes — the "naturality square."

In code: A natural transformation between two container types (say List→Maybe, taking the first element) must commute with map. head(list.map(f)) == f(head(list)). Naturality = "it doesn't matter if you transform before or after mapping."

6. Products & Coproducts — Universal Constructions

The product A×B is the "best" object that projects to both A and B. The coproduct A+B is the "best" object that both A and B inject into. "Best" means: any other candidate factors through it uniquely. This is a universal property.

🎮 Play: Universal Property

The product A×B sits at top. Any pretender C with maps to A and B must have a unique arrow to A×B. Drag C around — watch the unique arrow follow.

Drag the yellow node!
Products are everywhere: In sets: cartesian product. In types: tuples/structs. In logic: AND. In topology: product spaces. The same universal property defines all of them. Coproducts: disjoint union, Either type, OR, tagged unions. Category theory reveals AND and OR as dual — flip all arrows.

7. The Yoneda Lemma — You Are Your Relationships

The most profound result: an object A is completely determined by all the arrows into it (or out of it). The functor Hom(−, A) — which records, for every object X, all arrows X→A — captures everything about A. Two objects with the same hom-functor are isomorphic. An object is the totality of its relationships.

This is philosophy made precise: You don't need to know what something "really is." Its identity is entirely determined by how it relates to everything else. Electrons, people, data types — defined by interactions, not essence. Yoneda proves it.

8. Adjunctions — The Rosetta Stone

An adjunction L ⊣ R between categories C and D means: arrows L(A)→B in D correspond naturally to arrows A→R(B) in C. The left adjoint L is the "free" construction; the right adjoint R is the "forgetful" one. This is the deepest pattern in mathematics.

🎮 Play: Free ⊣ Forgetful

Left world: sets (just dots). Right world: groups (dots with structure). "Free" adds structure. "Forgetful" strips it. Watch the correspondence.

Adjunctions are everywhere: Free/forgetful (algebra), currying (λ-calculus), ∃⊣pullback⊣∀ (logic), tensor⊣hom (linear algebra), discretize⊣forget (topology). Saunders Mac Lane said: "Adjoint functors arise everywhere."

Everything is arrows.

You now have the vocabulary to see the universal patterns hiding in code, logic, physics, and thought. Welcome to the most abstract — and most practical — branch of mathematics.
