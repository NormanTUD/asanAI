① Persistence Diagram — The Starting Point

A persistence diagram is a multiset of points (b, d) where b = birth time and d = death time of a topological feature (like a loop or connected component). Points near the diagonal (b ≈ d) are "noise"; points far from the diagonal are significant features. Drag the points to see how they change!

How to read this: Each dot is a topological feature. The x-axis is when it's "born" and the y-axis is when it "dies." The further from the diagonal, the more persistent (important) the feature. Try dragging points toward or away from the diagonal!

② From Persistence Diagram → Persistent Landscape

Each point (b, d) in the persistence diagram generates a tent function: a triangle peaking at midpoint m = (b+d)/2 with height h = (d−b)/2. The k-th landscape function λₖ(t) is the k-th largest value among all tent functions at each t. The landscape is a functional summary of the diagram.

③ Wasserstein-1 Distance Between Two Diagrams

The Wasserstein-1 distance (a.k.a. Earth Mover's Distance for persistence diagrams) measures how much "work" it takes to transform one diagram into another. Each point can be matched to a point in the other diagram or projected to the diagonal. The cost is the sum of L∞ distances of the optimal matching.

Intuition: Imagine each red point is a pile of dirt and each blue point is a hole. W₁ measures the minimum total dirt × distance to fill all holes. Points with no match get projected to the diagonal (they "disappear"). A small W₁ means the diagrams are similar; a large W₁ means they are very different.

④ Wasserstein-1 via Landscapes: ∫|λᴬ(t) − λᴮ(t)| dt

Persistent landscapes give an alternative way to compare diagrams. The L¹ distance between landscapes is the integral of the absolute difference of the landscape functions. This is related to (but not identical to) the diagram-level Wasserstein distance, and is often easier to compute and has nice statistical properties.
