1. The Residual Stream: How Information Flows

A transformer's residual stream is like a river. Each layer adds its contribution to the stream. The final prediction is determined by the sum of ALL contributions from all layers.

💡 Key Insight: The final output is simply the sum of the initial embedding + all attention outputs + all FFN outputs. There's no nonlinearity between these additions!
L_out_final = L_in₀ + ATTN₀ + FFN₀ + ATTN₁ + FFN₁ + ... + ATTN₁₅ + FFN₁₅

2. Before-Softmax (BS) Values: The Core Mechanism

Each vector can be projected into vocabulary space to get a "before-softmax value" (bs-value) for every token. The softmax then converts these into probabilities.

p(w|x) = exp(bs_w) / Σ exp(bs_i)
💡 Key Insight: When you add two vectors, their bs-values ADD directly: bs(x+v) = bs(x) + bs(v). This is the fundamental mechanism of residual connections!

3. Distribution Change: Adding Vectors

When vector v is added to vector x, the resulting distribution depends on BOTH vectors' bs-values. Tokens with high bs-values in v will see their probability increase.

💡 Key Insight: It's not just about how large bs_v(w) is — it's about how much LARGER bs_v(w) is compared to other tokens' bs-values in v. A uniform v changes nothing!

Interactive: Adding Vector v to Vector x

Adjust the bs-values of vector v and see how the distribution of x+v changes. Try making v uniform — notice nothing changes!

4. Why Log-Probability Increase? The Fairness Problem

Using raw probability increase as a contribution score is unfair to lower layers. The sigmoid-like curve means equal bs-value additions cause tiny probability changes at low probabilities but huge changes at high probabilities.

F(l) = A·eˡ / (A·eˡ + B), where A = exp(bs_x(w)), B = Σ exp(bs_x(other))

Interactive: The Unfairness of Probability Increase

Adjust A/B ratio (representing how likely the target token already is) and see how the same bs-value addition l causes different probability increases. Log-probability gives a more linear (fair) curve.

5. Layer Exploration: Where is Knowledge Stored?

The paper finds that ALL layers contribute to prediction, but the log-probability curve is steep on lower layers and flatter on upper layers.

💡 Key Insight: Knowledge isn't stored in just one layer — it's distributed. Both attention AND FFN layers contribute. Many FFN subvalues work together (not just one or two).

6. Cross-Layer Interactions: Queries Activate Values

A vector doesn't just contribute as a "value" (directly affecting probabilities). It also acts as a "query" that activates FFN subvalues in upper layers by affecting their coefficient scores.

m_k = LN(L_res) · fc1_k + b_k
ffn_k = m_k · fc2_k
💡 Key Insight: Attention layers tend to INCREASE helpful subvalues' coefficients (positive inner products), while FFN layers tend to DECREASE unhelpful subvalues' coefficients. They work differently!

7. Case Study: "The capital of France is" → "Paris"

Let's trace how the model distinguishes "Paris" from "London" (another capital) and "Bordeaux" (another French city).

BS-Values Through the Residual Stream

Watch how bs-values for different cities evolve through the layers. "Paris" gradually separates from competitors.

Paris vs London

Separated mainly by attention layers 12-15, specifically the 4th attention subvalue (token "France"). The attention layers extract "Paris" from the "France" embedding.

Paris vs Bordeaux

Separated mainly by FFN layers 11-13. These FFN subvalues store knowledge like "Paris is a capital/city" with top tokens being city names.

💡 Final Insight: Knowledge isn't stored as a single fact ⟨France, capital, Paris⟩. Instead:
• FFN layers store "Paris is a capital/city" (activated by attention subvalues related to "capitals/cities")
• Attention layers extract "Paris" from the "France" token embedding
Both mechanisms work together!
