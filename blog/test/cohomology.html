Persistent Cohomology

Persistent Cohomology is the algebraic dual of persistent homology. While homology detects "holes" by finding cycles (chains with no boundary), cohomology assigns values to those cycles — it's about functions on topology rather than the topology itself.

Key Duality: Homology asks "what holes exist?" — Cohomology asks "what functions can I define on those holes?"
Practical Payoff: Cohomology gives us the cup product (extra algebraic structure) and enables computation of circular coordinates on data — mapping data points onto a circle that respects the topology.

The Cup Product — Extra Structure from Cohomology

Cohomology carries a ring structure via the cup product: given two cocycles α and β, their cup product α ∪ β detects interactions between holes. This is invisible to homology alone!

Example: A torus T² has two independent 1-cycles (a and b). Homology sees them both. But the cup product α ∪ β ≠ 0 tells us these cycles are linked — they form the torus structure. On a wedge of two circles (figure-8), α ∪ β = 0 even though both cycles exist — they don't interact.

Multiparameter Persistent Homology

Multiparameter persistence extends classical persistent homology by considering two or more filtration parameters simultaneously. Instead of a single scale parameter ε, we have a multi-dimensional parameter space.

Why? A single filtration often doesn't capture all structure. For example, you might want to filter by both spatial scale (ε) and density (ρ) simultaneously. This creates a bifiltration — a 2D grid of simplicial complexes.

Challenge: Unlike 1-parameter persistence, there is no complete barcode decomposition for multiparameter persistence modules. The algebraic structure is fundamentally richer and harder to summarize.

Fibered Barcode & Persistence Along Lines

Since multiparameter persistence has no single barcode, one approach is to restrict to 1-dimensional lines through the parameter space and compute ordinary persistence along each line. The collection of all such barcodes is called the fibered barcode.

Idea: Pick a direction (angle θ) in the (ε, ρ) parameter plane. Walk along that line and record births/deaths of topological features. Different angles reveal different aspects of the data's multi-scale structure.
