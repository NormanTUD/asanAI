1. Cartesian Products — The Starting Point

Before fiber bundles, you need Cartesian products. If you have a set A and a set B, their product A × B is just all pairs (a, b). Geometrically: a circle × a line = a cylinder. A circle × a circle = a torus.

2. What is a Fiber?

Imagine you're an ant walking along a path (the base space). At every point of your path, there's a pole sticking up (the fiber). You can climb up and down the pole, but you can also walk along the path. The total collection of all poles along the path is the total space.

Formally: a fiber bundle (E, B, π, F) consists of a total space E, a base space B, a projection map π: E → B, and a fiber F such that π⁻¹(b) ≅ F for every point b in B.

3. Trivial Bundles

A trivial bundle is just a Cartesian product: E = B × F. The cylinder S¹ × [0,1] is a trivial bundle over the circle. Nothing twists. Every local piece looks like the global picture.

4. The Möbius Strip — Your First Non-Trivial Bundle

Take a strip of paper. Give it a half-twist and glue the ends. You get a Möbius strip. Locally, every small piece looks like a rectangle (base × fiber). But globally? It's twisted. You cannot write E = B × F. This is a non-trivial fiber bundle over S¹ with fiber [−1, 1].

Mind-blower: An ant walking around the Möbius strip returns to its starting point upside down. It must go around twice to return to its original orientation. This is the essence of non-trivial topology.

5. Cylinder vs. Möbius — Side by Side

Both are fiber bundles over S¹ with fiber [−1,1]. Both look identical locally. The difference is global topology — how the fibers are glued together as you go around.

🎮 Play: Parallel Transport Comparison

Watch a vector get "parallel transported" around each bundle. On the cylinder it returns unchanged. On the Möbius strip it flips.

This "flip" is detected by the structure group of the bundle. For the Möbius strip, the structure group is ℤ₂ = {+1, −1}. The transition function between overlapping local patches multiplies the fiber coordinate by −1.

6. The Formal Definition

A fiber bundle is a tuple (E, B, π, F, G) where:

  • E = total space (the whole thing)
  • B = base space (where you walk)
  • π : E → B = projection (drops you down to the base)
  • F = fiber (what's attached at each point)
  • G = structure group (symmetries of F that encode twisting)

Local triviality: For every b ∈ B, there exists a neighborhood U and a homeomorphism φ: π⁻¹(U) → U × F such that the diagram commutes with π.

🎮 Play: Local vs Global

Click patches on the circle to see local trivializations. Notice how they agree on overlaps (cylinder) or disagree by a sign (Möbius).

The transition functions g_αβ : U_α ∩ U_β → G tell you how to translate between local descriptions. They satisfy the cocycle condition: g_αβ · g_βγ = g_αγ on triple overlaps. This is the algebraic heart of fiber bundles.

7. The Hopf Fibration — The Crown Jewel

The Hopf fibration is one of the most beautiful objects in mathematics. It's a fiber bundle:

S¹ → S³ → S²

The 3-sphere S³ fibers over the 2-sphere S² with circular fibers S¹. Every point on the 2-sphere has a circle living above it in 4D, and these circles are all linked — every pair of fibers forms a Hopf link. Discovered by Heinz Hopf in 1931, it was the first example showing that π₃(S²) ≠ 0.

Mind-blower: The Hopf fibration appears in quantum mechanics! The state space of a qubit is S³ (up to phase), and the observable states live on the Bloch sphere S². The Hopf map is exactly the projection from quantum states to measurement outcomes.

8. Connections & Parallel Transport

A connection on a fiber bundle tells you how to move "horizontally" — how to transport information along the base while staying consistent in the fiber. Without a connection, there's no canonical way to compare fibers at different points.

Think of it this way: you're on a curved surface carrying a compass needle. As you walk, the needle rotates relative to your local frame. The holonomy — the total rotation after a closed loop — measures the curvature of the connection.

In physics, connections on fiber bundles are gauge fields. The electromagnetic potential A_μ is a connection on a U(1) bundle. The Yang-Mills fields of the Standard Model are connections on SU(2) × U(1) and SU(3) bundles. Curvature = field strength.

9. Why Fiber Bundles Matter

🔬 Physics

Electromagnetism = U(1) bundle over spacetime. General relativity = frame bundle over spacetime. The Standard Model = SU(3)×SU(2)×U(1) bundle. Berry phase in quantum mechanics = holonomy of a connection. Magnetic monopoles = non-trivial U(1) bundles over S².

📐 Mathematics

Characteristic classes (Chern, Stiefel-Whitney, Pontryagin) are topological invariants that detect non-triviality. The tangent bundle of any manifold is a fiber bundle. The hairy ball theorem says the tangent bundle of S² is non-trivial!

The hairy ball theorem: You cannot comb a hairy sphere flat without creating a cowlick. Formally: S² admits no non-vanishing continuous tangent vector field. This means the tangent bundle TS² is non-trivial — it's not S² × ℝ². Try it with a tennis ball!
