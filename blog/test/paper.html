Transformer Layers as Fibre Bundle Morphisms

An interactive exploration of the conjecture that transformer layers don't move points through a fixed space — they morph the space itself via Lipschitz-continuous maps, forming a fibre bundle whose geometry encodes the model's reasoning.

Core Idea

In general relativity, gravity doesn't push objects through space — it changes the geometry of space itself, and objects follow geodesics. This paper proposes the same reinterpretation for transformers: attention and feed-forward layers morph the representation space via Lipschitz-continuous maps, and the information content of the model is encoded in the geometry of those morphings.
Fibre Bundle Structure: The base manifold M is the set of token positions {1,…,n}. Over each position sits a fibre — the stack of representation spaces across all L layers. Each layer defines a map Φ(ℓ) : ℝd → ℝd that acts on every fibre simultaneously.

Conjectures

C1 — Space-Morphing, Not Point-Moving

A transformer layer is better described as a deformation of the embedding space than as a displacement of individual token vectors. The geometry of the map — its Jacobian field, eigenvalue spectrum, divergence, curl, and shear — provides access to the model's reasoning.

C2 — Holographic Scrambling

Due to polysemanticity, information about any single concept is spread across many dimensions and layers — like a hologram where removing a part reduces resolution but doesn't remove any single region of the image entirely.

C3 — Topological Computation

The scrambled representations contain topological structures (persistent loops, helices, spirals) that perform the model's actual computations — analogous to states on a Turing machine tape.

C4 — Inner Layers Compute, Outer Layers Translate

Middle layers perform the bulk of geometric computation (large deformations, high curvature), while first and last layers translate between token-embedding space and the model's internal geometric language. The last layer often morphs space back toward the embedding geometry.

Curvature Across Layers

Beyond per-layer Jacobians, the fibre bundle gives rise to curvature that measures how geometry changes across layers. This view shows Ollivier–Ricci curvature (token convergence/divergence), scalar curvature (volume change), and Procrustes deviation (frame rotation) as functions of layer depth.
Notice the characteristic pattern: curvature peaks in the middle layers (where the model is "computing" in its internal geometric language) and drops at the boundaries (where it translates to/from token space). This supports Conjecture C4.

Residual Stream — Wave of Deformations

The residual connection h(ℓ+1) = h(ℓ) + Δ(ℓ) means each layer contributes an additive delta. The cumulative effect is a superposition of deformations — a "wave" of space-morphing propagating from layer 0 to layer L. Watch the deltas accumulate.
Each colored line is one dimension of the representation vector. The cumulative view shows the running sum; the delta view shows each layer's individual contribution. The "wave" is the collective pattern of these deltas propagating through the layer stack.

Persistent Homology — Topological Structures

Conjecture C3 posits that topological structures (loops, voids, connected components) in the representation space perform computation. This view shows a simulated persistence barcode: each bar represents a topological feature that is "born" at some filtration scale and "dies" at another. Long bars = robust features.
Long bars indicate robust topological features that persist across scales — these are candidates for the "computational structures" described in Conjecture C3. Notice how the barcode changes across layers: middle layers tend to have more complex topology.
