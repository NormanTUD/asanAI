Step 0 — The Big Idea

What if neural networks don't move points — they bend space?

🍎 Gravity analogy: Einstein showed that gravity doesn't "pull" objects through space — it curves space itself, and objects follow the curves. This paper proposes the same idea for AI: transformer layers don't push token vectors around a fixed space — they warp the space those vectors live in.

In a standard transformer (like GPT), each word in your sentence becomes a vector — a list of numbers. As it passes through each layer, those numbers change. The usual story is: "the layer moved the point." The new story is: "the layer reshaped the entire space, and the point just went along for the ride."

Step 1 — Fibre Bundles (Don't Panic)

What's a Fibre Bundle?

🏢 Building analogy: Imagine a skyscraper where each floor is a different "version" of the same room. The building's address is the base (which token position we're talking about). Each floor is a fibre (the representation at a particular layer). The whole building is the fibre bundle. An elevator ride from floor to floor is a layer map.

In a transformer with L layers and n tokens:

  • Base space: The token positions {1, 2, 3, …, n} — like addresses on a street
  • Fibre: At each position, a stack of representation spaces — one per layer — like floors in a building
  • Layer map: The function that transforms floor ℓ into floor ℓ+1 — the "elevator" that reshapes the room as you ride it

Step 2 — The Jacobian: X-Ray of a Warp

How Do We Measure Space-Warping?

🔬 Microscope analogy: If you zoom in on a tiny patch of the warped space, it looks almost like a simple linear stretch/rotate/squish. The Jacobian matrix captures exactly that local stretch-rotate-squish. It's like putting the warp under a microscope.

The Jacobian is a matrix J that tells you, at every point in space, how the warp behaves locally. From it we extract:

📊 Divergence (trace of J)

Is the space expanding or contracting at this point? Positive = expansion, negative = contraction.

🌀 Curl (antisymmetric part)

Is the space rotating at this point? High curl = lots of swirling, mixing information between dimensions.

📐 Shear (traceless symmetric part)

Is the space being stretched unevenly? Like pulling taffy — some directions stretch while others compress, but the total volume stays the same.

📏 Determinant

The overall volume change. det > 1 means expansion, 0 < det < 1 means contraction, det < 0 means the space got flipped inside-out!

Step 6 — The Residual Stream

Waves of Warping

🌊 Wave analogy: Each layer adds a small "delta" warp to a running total. It's like waves on the ocean — each wave adds to the surface, and the final shape is the sum of all waves. The residual connection (skip connection) is the ocean surface that carries all the waves.

Mathematically: h[layer+1] = h[layer] + Δ[layer]. The Jacobian of this is J = I + J_delta, and the proven property tells us: tr(I + J_delta) = n + tr(J_delta) — the divergence of the full layer is the dimension plus the divergence of just the delta.

Step 7 — Inner vs. Outer Layers

The Middle Does the Heavy Lifting

🏭 Factory analogy: The first layers are the "loading dock" — they translate raw input into the factory's internal language. The middle layers are the "assembly line" — where the real work happens, with big, complex transformations. The last layers are the "shipping department" — they translate the result back into a form the outside world can read.

Step 8 — Holographic Scrambling

Information is Everywhere (and Nowhere)

🌈 Hologram analogy: Cut a hologram in half and you still see the whole image — just blurrier. Similarly, in a transformer, the concept "cat" isn't stored in one neuron — it's spread across many neurons and many layers. Remove some neurons and you lose resolution, but not the concept entirely. This is polysemanticity: each neuron participates in encoding many concepts.

Key Takeaways

  • Transformer layers warp space — they don't just move points, they reshape the entire geometry of the representation space.
  • The Jacobian captures the warp locally — it decomposes cleanly into symmetric (stretch/shear) and antisymmetric (rotation) parts, proven orthogonal in Coq.
  • Divergence comes only from strain — rotation doesn't change volume. The antisymmetric part is always trace-free.
  • Shear is pure shape-change — it distorts without changing volume (also trace-free, proven in Coq).
  • The residual stream accumulates warps — each layer adds a delta, and the identity contributes dimension n to the divergence.
  • Inner layers do the heavy lifting — outer layers translate between human-readable and internal representations.
  • Information is holographically distributed — like a hologram, removing parts degrades gracefully rather than catastrophically.
