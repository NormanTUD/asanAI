Kettenkomplexe

An interactive visual guide to chain complexes, cochain complexes, homology, double complexes, chain maps, and the Euler characteristic.

1. Chain Complex (Kettenkomplex)

A sequence of abelian groups (or R-modules) connected by boundary operators dn such that dn ∘ dn+1 = 0.

n-Chains

Elements of each module Cn in the sequence.

x ∈ Cn

n-Cycles (Zykel)

Elements in the kernel of dn.

Zn = ker dn ⊆ Cn

n-Boundaries (Ränder)

Elements in the image of dn+1.

Bn = im dn+1 ⊆ Cn

Homology Hn

Cycles modulo boundaries — measures failure of exactness.

Hn = Zn / Bn

2. Cochain Complex (Kokettenkomplex)

Like a chain complex, but the maps go "upward": dn: Cn → Cn+1, with dn ∘ dn−1 = 0.

n-Cocycles (Kozykel)

Zn = ker dn ⊆ Cn

n-Coboundaries (Koränder)

Bn = im dn−1 ⊆ Cn

Cohomology Hn

Hn = Zn / Bn

4. Chain Map (Kettenhomomorphismus)

A chain map f: (A, dA) → (B, dB) is a family of maps fn: An → Bn commuting with the boundary operators: dB,n ∘ fn = fn−1 ∘ dA,n.

Commutativity Condition

The diagram commutes: going right then down equals going down then right.

dB,n ∘ fn = fn−1 ∘ dA,n

This ensures f maps cycles to cycles and boundaries to boundaries, so it induces a map on homology.

5. Double Complex (Doppelkomplex)

A grid of objects Dp,q with horizontal maps d and vertical maps d', satisfying d∘d = 0, d'∘d' = 0, and d∘d' + d'∘d = 0.

Hover over cells to highlight the diagonal (total complex degree). Click a cell to see its maps.

Hover over a cell to see details.

Total Complex Tot(D)n

The total complex collapses the double complex into a single chain complex by summing along anti-diagonals:

Tot(D)n = ⊕p+q=n Dp,q

The boundary map on an element x ∈ Dp,q is:

dn(x) = d(x) + d'(x)

The anti-commutativity condition d∘d' + d'∘d = 0 ensures dn ∘ dn+1 = 0.

6. Euler Characteristic (Euler-Charakteristik)

For a cochain complex with finite-dimensional cohomology, χ = Σ (−1)i dim Hi. Equivalently, χ = Σ (−1)i dim Ci.

Special case: For a complex C0 → C1 (a single map f), χ = dim ker f − dim coker f, which is the Fredholm index.

7. Examples

Classical appearances of chain and cochain complexes.

Simplicial Complex

The chain groups are generated by simplices of each dimension. The boundary operator maps an n-simplex to the alternating sum of its (n−1)-faces.

Singular Homology

The singular chain complex of a topological space X uses continuous maps from standard simplices into X. Its homology captures "holes" in X.

Group (Co)homology

For a group G and a G-module M, one constructs a (co)chain complex whose (co)homology groups are the group (co)homology Hn(G, M).

de Rham Complex

On a smooth manifold, the exterior derivative d gives a cochain complex of differential forms: Ω0 → Ω1 → Ω2 → ⋯

Single Morphism f: A → B

Any homomorphism defines a two-term complex. H0 = ker f, H1 = coker f. The Euler characteristic is the Fredholm index.

Elliptic / Dirac Complex

A cochain complex of differential operators on a manifold, important in global analysis and the Atiyah-Singer index theorem.
