What is Group Theory?

An interactive visual exploration of one of math's most beautiful ideas.

① Symmetries of a Square

A group captures the idea of symmetry. Take a square: you can rotate it by 0°, 90°, 180°, or 270°, and flip it across 4 axes — that's 8 symmetries total. Each one leaves the square looking the same. Click the buttons to apply transformations and watch the labeled corners move!

② The Four Group Axioms

For a set with an operation to be a group, it must satisfy exactly four rules. Click each axiom below to see it demonstrated with our square symmetries.

🔒

Closure

Combining any two symmetries gives another symmetry in the set.

🔗

Associativity

(a ∘ b) ∘ c = a ∘ (b ∘ c) — grouping doesn't matter.

🪞

Identity

There's a "do nothing" element (R0) that leaves everything unchanged.

↩️

Inverses

Every symmetry can be undone by another symmetry.

This table shows what happens when you combine any two symmetries of the square. Hover over a cell to see "row ∘ column = result". Notice: the table is not symmetric across the diagonal — this group is non-abelian (order matters)!

⑥ What's Next?

You've just scratched the surface! Group theory underpins physics (particle symmetries), chemistry (molecular symmetries), cryptography (elliptic curves), music theory (transpositions), and Rubik's cube solutions. Every time you see symmetry or structure-preserving transformations, there's a group hiding underneath.
