Fourier Projection: Extracting Circles from Skewed Embedding Spaces

1. The Problem: Circles Live "Skewed" in High Dimensions

In the grokking paper, transformers trained on modular addition learn to embed inputs onto circles in the embedding space. The key insight is that these circles don't align neatly with any 2D coordinate plane — they exist in a skewed orientation across multiple dimensions.

2. Why Simple Slicing Fails

If we try to look at the data by projecting onto standard coordinate planes (XY, XZ, YZ), we see ellipses or distorted shapes — not clean circles. The circular structure is "spread out" across all three dimensions.

This is exactly what happens in neural network embeddings: the periodic structure exists in the Fourier basis, not in the standard neuron basis.

⚠️ The Problem: Standard axis-aligned projections distort the circle into ellipses. We lose the key structural information — the periodicity and uniform spacing of points.

3. The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) — The Key Tool

The Discrete Fourier Transform decomposes a signal into its frequency components. For a sequence of P values, the DFT finds how much of each frequency is present.

In the grokking context, the embedding matrix maps inputs to sines and cosines at specific frequencies. The DFT lets us identify which frequencies are active.

8. Summary: Why Fourier Projection Works

The core idea:

  1. Circles in embedding space don't align with coordinate axes — they're "skewed" across many dimensions.
  2. The DFT provides an orthogonal basis of sinusoidal functions at different frequencies.
  3. Fourier projection = computing the inner product of the data with sin/cos at each frequency. This extracts the amplitude of circular motion at that frequency.
  4. The Fourier coefficients at a given frequency k define the 2D plane in which the circle at that frequency lives.
  5. Multiple frequencies can be superimposed — the DFT separates them cleanly because sin/cos at different frequencies are orthogonal.
