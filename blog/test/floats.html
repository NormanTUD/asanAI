What is a Floating Point Number?

Computers can only store 0s and 1s. So how do they store numbers like 3.14 or -0.001? They use a system called IEEE 754 Floating Point, which is similar to scientific notation:

value = (-1)^sign × 1.mantissa × 2^(exponent - bias)

Every floating point number is split into 3 parts:

Sign (1 bit) — Is the number positive (0) or negative (1)?

Exponent (8 or 11 bits) — How big or small is the number? (like the power of 10 in scientific notation, but using power of 2)

Mantissa/Fraction (23 or 52 bits) — The precise digits of the number

Famous Precision Problems

Because floats use binary fractions, many simple decimal numbers cannot be stored exactly. It's like how 1/3 = 0.3333... never ends in decimal — similarly, 0.1 never ends in binary!

0.1 + 0.2 = not exactly 0.3!
0.1 in binary = 0.0001100110011001100... (repeats forever)
1/3 in decimal = 0.3333333333333... (same idea!)
9007199254740992 + 1 = integers lose precision too!

Float Density: Numbers Aren't Evenly Spaced!

Unlike integers, floating point numbers are not evenly distributed. They are packed very densely near zero and become more spread out as they get larger. Between 1 and 2, there are the same number of floats as between 2 and 4, or between 4 and 8!
