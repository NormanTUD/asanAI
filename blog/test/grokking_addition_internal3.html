How a Tiny AI Learned to Add
A visual explanation of the "Grokking" paper for curious humans

The Setup: Researchers trained a tiny neural network (a "transformer") to compute things like 17 + 42 mod 113 = 59. "Mod 113" just means: if the answer goes past 112, wrap around back to 0 (like a clock with 113 hours instead of 12).

The network has 128 dimensions internally, uses 4 attention heads, and has 512 neurons in its MLP layer. It was trained on only 30% of all possible input pairs.

The surprise: The network first memorized all the answers like flashcards. Then, after thousands more training steps, it suddenly "got it" — it discovered a beautiful mathematical trick that lets it compute ANY answer, even ones it never saw during training.

This document explains what that trick is and how each part of the network contributes.

Chapter 1: The Clock Trick — Why Circles?

Here is the single most important insight of the whole paper:

Adding numbers mod 113 is the same as rotating a point around a circle. If you place the numbers 0 through 112 evenly around a circle, then "adding b" means "rotating by b steps." The answer is wherever you land.
Imagine a clock with 113 tick marks (numbered 0 to 112). You start at tick mark "a". You rotate forward by "b" tick marks. Wherever the hand points — that's (a + b) mod 113.

But how do you describe "where you are on a circle" with numbers a computer can work with? That's where cosine and sine come in.

Cosine and Sine = "Address on a Circle"

Think of a circle drawn on graph paper. Any point on the circle has an x-coordinate and a y-coordinate:

  • Cosine = how far RIGHT the point is (the x-coordinate)
  • Sine = how far UP the point is (the y-coordinate)

Together, (cos, sin) tells you EXACTLY where on the circle you are. It's like GPS coordinates, but for a circle.

What does "frequency k" mean? It controls how fast numbers move around the circle. At frequency k=1, the numbers 0 to 112 make exactly ONE full trip around the circle. At k=2, they make TWO full trips (so each number appears at two different spots that overlap). At k=14, they make 14 full trips.

Think of a vinyl record. Frequency k=1 is like one groove going around once. Frequency k=14 is like a groove that spirals around 14 times. Different frequencies give you different "views" of the same set of numbers — like looking at the same city from 5 different hilltops.

The network uses 5 specific frequencies: k = 14, 35, 41, 42, and 52. Why 5? We'll get to that in Chapter 4.

Chapter 2: The Addition Formula — How to Add Without Adding

Here's the mathematical magic that makes everything work. There's a formula from trigonometry (discovered centuries ago) that says:

cos(angle_A + angle_B) = cos(angle_A) × cos(angle_B) − sin(angle_A) × sin(angle_B)

Let me translate this into plain English:

If you know WHERE "a" is on the circle (its cos and sin),

and you know WHERE "b" is on the circle (its cos and sin),

then you can figure out WHERE "a+b" is on the circle — just by multiplying and subtracting the coordinates you already have!

You never need to actually "add" a and b as numbers. You just combine their circle-positions using multiplication and subtraction.

The formula needs us to MULTIPLY cos(a) with cos(b), and sin(a) with sin(b). Neural networks are mostly about adding things together (linear operations). So where does the multiplication come from? That's what the attention mechanism provides — it's the only part of the network that can multiply two different inputs together.

Chapter 3: The Full Pipeline — What Each Part Does

Now let's see how the transformer's components work together. Here's the flow:

→
Attentionmultiplies sin/cos
→
MLPapplies the formula
→
Unembeddingscores each answer
→
Softmaxpicks the winner

Let me explain each one:

1

Embedding — "Put the numbers on circles"

The embedding is a big table (128 rows × 113 columns). Each column stores the "circle coordinates" for one number. After training, column "a" basically contains cos(w×a) and sin(w×a) for each of the 5 key frequencies.

Think of it as: A lookup table. You give it the number 42, it gives you back 10 values: the cos and sin of 42 at each of the 5 frequencies.

Where is sine "stored"? RIGHT HERE. The sine and cosine values ARE the learned weights of this matrix. The network figured out during training that storing numbers as circle-coordinates is useful.

2

Attention — "Multiply the coordinates together"

Remember: the addition formula needs products like cos(a)×cos(b). But the network mostly does addition (linear operations). Attention is special because it's bilinear — it naturally creates products.

How? The attention mechanism computes a "weight" that depends on token a, and multiplies it by a "value" that depends on token b. Weight × Value = a product of functions of a and b. That's exactly the multiplication we need!

Specifically: The attention weight from the "=" token to token "a" is approximately:

weight ≈ 0.5 + 0.27 × (cos(w×a) − cos(w×b))

This weight multiplies the value (which contains cos(w×b) and sin(w×b)), creating the cross-terms cos(w×a)×cos(w×b) etc.

Important detail: The softmax in attention is operating almost linearly here (97.5% of its behavior is explained by a straight line). This is what makes the whole thing work cleanly as a multiplication.

3

MLP (Feed-Forward Network) — "Apply the addition formula"

The MLP takes the products created by attention and combines them according to the trig formula. Each neuron computes something like:

ReLU( weight₁ × cos(a)cos(b) + weight₂ × sin(a)sin(b) + ... )

The result: the MLP outputs values proportional to cos(w×(a+b)) and sin(w×(a+b)) — the circle-coordinates of the SUM.

How well does this work? 84.6% of the 512 neurons (that's 433 neurons) have over 85% of their behavior explained by a simple formula involving just ONE frequency. The neurons are specialized!

4

Unembedding — "Score every possible answer"

Now the network has cos(w×(a+b)) and sin(w×(a+b)). But it needs to output a specific NUMBER (0 to 112). How?

The unembedding matrix computes, for each possible answer c:

score(c) = cos(w×(a+b)) × cos(w×c) + sin(w×(a+b)) × sin(w×c)

This simplifies (by another trig identity) to:

score(c) = cos(w × (a+b−c))

When c equals the correct answer (a+b mod 113), then (a+b−c) = 0, and cos(0) = 1 (the maximum!). For wrong answers, cos gives smaller values.

5

Softmax — "Pick the highest score"

Softmax takes all 113 scores and converts them to probabilities. The highest score gets almost all the probability. Since the correct answer has the highest score (thanks to constructive interference), the network outputs the right answer.

Chapter 4: Why 5 Frequencies? — The Interference Trick

Here's a problem: with just ONE frequency, cos(w×(a+b−c)) is maximized at the correct c, but it's ALSO nearly 1 at some wrong answers. For example, with k=14, the value at c=correct is 1.000, but at c=correct±8 it's 0.998 — almost indistinguishable!

Imagine trying to find one specific person in a crowd using only ONE feature ("wears glasses"). Many people wear glasses! But if you use FIVE features ("glasses + red jacket + tall + beard + hat"), only ONE person matches all five.

The network adds up cos waves from 5 different frequencies. At the correct answer, ALL FIVE waves equal 1 (because cos(0)=1 for all of them). At any wrong answer, the waves point in random directions and mostly cancel out.

Chapter 6: How the Network Learned This (Grokking)

The network didn't figure this out instantly. Training happened in three phases:

1

Memorization (epochs 0 – 1,400)

The network just memorizes the training examples like flashcards. It gets 100% on training data but 0% on new data. The Fourier trick hasn't formed yet.

2

Circuit Formation (epochs 1,400 – 9,400)

Slowly, in the background, the Fourier trick starts forming. The weights gradually become more "sine-like." But the memorized answers are still there too, so test accuracy doesn't improve yet. You can't see the progress from outside!

3

Cleanup (epochs 9,400 – 14,000)

Weight decay (a training rule that says "make all weights a little smaller each step") gradually erases the memorized junk. The Fourier circuit is compact and survives; the memorization is bulky and gets erased. Suddenly, test accuracy shoots to 100%!

A student memorizes 1000 math problems. Meanwhile, they're unconsciously noticing the pattern. The teacher gradually shrinks their cheat sheet (weight decay). Eventually the cheat sheet is too small to hold all the memorized answers, but the pattern still fits — so the student is forced to use the pattern. Suddenly they can solve ANY problem!

Chapter 7: Frequently Asked Questions

Q: "Where is the sine generated?"

A: It's NOT generated at runtime. The sine values are literally the numbers stored in the embedding matrix's weights. During training, the network learned that storing numbers as (cos, sin) pairs is the best strategy. The embedding matrix IS a table of pre-computed sines and cosines.

Q: "How does the addition formula actually get computed?"

A: In two steps: (1) Attention creates the products cos(a)×cos(b) and sin(a)×sin(b) by multiplying attention-weights (which depend on a) with values (which depend on b). (2) The MLP combines these products with learned weights that implement the subtraction: cos(a)cos(b) MINUS sin(a)sin(b) = cos(a+b).

Q: "What does the ReLU activation do?"

A: ReLU (which zeros out negative values) is the nonlinearity in the MLP. Each neuron computes a weighted sum of the products from attention, applies ReLU, and the result approximates a piece of the trig formula. Multiple neurons working together reconstruct the full cos(w×(a+b)) signal. 84.6% of neurons are well-described by a degree-2 polynomial of a single frequency.

Q: "Why these specific 5 frequencies (14, 35, 41, 42, 52)?"

A: There's nothing mathematically special about these exact numbers. Different random seeds produce different key frequencies (e.g., one seed used k=2, 9, 19, 31). What matters is that the chosen frequencies, when their cosines are added together, create strong constructive interference at x=0 and destructive interference everywhere else. The network finds whichever set of frequencies achieves this during training.

Q: "Why does weight decay matter?"

A: Without weight decay, the network never "groks" — it stays memorized forever. Weight decay constantly pushes all weights toward zero. The memorization solution needs LOTS of weight (one entry per training example). The Fourier solution is compact (just 5 frequencies). So weight decay selectively destroys memorization while preserving the elegant algorithm.

Appendix: Quick Reference Card

The problem: Compute (a + b) mod 113, where a and b are integers from 0 to 112.

The network: 1-layer transformer, 128-dim embeddings, 4 attention heads (dim 32 each), 512 MLP neurons, ReLU activation, trained with weight decay λ=1.

The algorithm it discovers:

  1. Embed a and b as (cos(ω_k·a), sin(ω_k·a)) and (cos(ω_k·b), sin(ω_k·b)) for k ∈ {14, 35, 41, 42, 52}
  2. Use attention (bilinear) to create products: cos(ω·a)·cos(ω·b), sin(ω·a)·sin(ω·b), etc.
  3. Use MLP to combine products via trig identity → cos(ω·(a+b)), sin(ω·(a+b))
  4. Unembed: for each candidate c, compute score = Σ_k α_k · cos(ω_k·(a+b−c))
  5. Softmax picks the c with the highest score (which is c* = (a+b) mod 113)

Key numbers from the paper:

  • 5 key frequencies explain 95% of logit variance
  • 84.6% of MLP neurons are well-described by a single-frequency degree-2 polynomial
  • Softmax in attention is 97.5% linear
  • Ablating non-key frequencies IMPROVES performance (loss drops 70%)
  • Grokking phases: memorization (0–1.4k epochs), circuit formation (1.4k–9.4k), cleanup (9.4k–14k)
