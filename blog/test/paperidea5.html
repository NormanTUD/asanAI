1. The Blurry World Map — Embedding as Lowest Resolution

Imagine the model's entire knowledge as a landscape of islands. Each island is a topic — math, poetry, biology, programming. The embedding layer (layer 0) sees all of them at once, but everything is blurry. It's like looking at a world map from space: you can see continents, but no streets.

The embedding is the "global section" — the coarsest, lowest-resolution image of everything the model knows. Every dimension contributes to every concept, like a hologram where every piece contains the whole picture, just blurrier.

Click any island to focus attention on it. Click again to zoom deeper. The focused area sharpens while everything else stays blurry — that's the resolution–breadth tradeoff.

2. Zooming In — Layers as Progressive Resolution

Each transformer layer is like adjusting the zoom on a microscope. Early layers do coarse routing — "this sentence is about math, not poetry." Middle layers resolve finer detail — "this is about calculus, specifically integrals." Late layers produce the sharpest local picture.

The model doesn't move tokens through a fixed space — it morphs the space itself. Each layer warps the geometry so that relevant regions expand and irrelevant ones compress. The grid deformation below IS the computation.

3. Multiple Islands — Parallel Activation with Holographic Icons

When you ask a model to "write a Python function that computes Fibonacci numbers," it needs multiple islands active simultaneously: programming, mathematics, and language. Each island encodes a topological icon — a torus for programming, a loop space for math, a spiral for history — decomposed via 2D DCT just like a hologram. Focusing lenses on an island adds more frequency tiles, sharpening its icon while the global budget forces others to blur.

Attention heads act as separate lenses — each one focuses on a different region. Where lenses overlap, resolution compounds. Where no lens looks, the landscape stays pixelated. The fixed dimension d limits how many islands can be sharp at once. Each icon is holographically encoded: every DCT tile contains the whole image, just blurrier — exactly like a real hologram.

Drag the colored circles to reposition attention lenses. Notice: making one area sharper forces others to blur — that's the finite-dimension tradeoff. Watch the topological icons sharpen and pixelate!

4. Parallel Transport — Moving Information Between Islands

The magic happens when information from one island flows to another. "Parallel transport" in the model means that patterns learned in one region (say, the structure of a for-loop) can be carried over to help resolve another region (say, mathematical recursion). The residual stream is the highway connecting all islands.

Each layer's residual connection h^(ℓ+1) = h^(ℓ) + δ^(ℓ) is a communication channel. The attention sub-layer decides WHICH information to transport between islands. The feed-forward sub-layer decides HOW to use it locally. Together they let the model "virtually zoom further" by borrowing resolution from related regions.

5. Hallucinations — Where the Map Lies

Hallucinations happen at the edges of the map. When the model zooms into a region where training data was sparse, it extrapolates — filling in the blank with patterns that are geometrically smooth and internally consistent, but factually wrong. The confidence stays high because the local geometry looks fine; it just doesn't correspond to reality.

Think of a satellite map with great data for Europe but sparse data for central Africa. Zoom in there and the system invents plausible terrain — rivers that don't exist. The model's "meaning field" is an approximation, and at the borders of well-learned patches, that approximation breaks down while still looking confident.

Move your mouse into the red zones — notice how confidence stays high even where knowledge is thin. That gap between confidence and coverage IS the hallucination.
