Transformer Dynamics

A neuroscientific approach to understanding how Large Language Models compute — treating the residual stream as a dynamical system evolving across layers.

Fernando & Guitchounts, 2025 • Llama 3.1 8B

🧠 The Big Idea

Just as neuroscientists study the brain by treating neural populations as dynamical systems evolving over time, this paper treats the transformer's residual stream as a dynamical system evolving over layers.
🔬 Analogy: Imagine watching a ball roll through a landscape. Instead of asking "what does each blade of grass do?", we ask "what is the ball's trajectory? Does it speed up? Does it spiral? Does it get pulled toward certain paths?" That's what this paper does for the information flowing through a transformer.

📈 Finding 1: Activations Grow Dense Over Layers

As information passes through the transformer, the activations of individual units in the residual stream systematically increase in magnitude. Units tend to preserve their sign (positive stays positive, negative stays negative), and the overall representation becomes "denser" — more units become strongly active.
Key Insight: The residual stream doesn't just passively carry information — it actively amplifies certain representational directions as it progresses through layers.

🔗 Finding 2: Strong Continuity Despite No Privileged Basis

A surprising finding: individual units in the residual stream maintain strong correlations between consecutive layers (r > 0.8 even in early layers). This is unexpected because the residual stream doesn't have a "privileged basis" — the coordinate axes aren't supposed to be meaningful. Yet individual coordinates behave coherently!
Within-layer transitions (Attention → MLP within the same layer) show higher correlations than cross-layer transitions (MLP → next layer's Attention).
This suggests attention operations make smaller changes to the RS vectors than the cross-layer transitions.
Cosine similarity between consecutive RS vectors also increases over layers — the direction of the full vector becomes increasingly stable.

🚀 Finding 3: The Residual Stream Accelerates

The velocity of the residual stream — how much the representation changes from one layer to the next — shows a distinct pattern of acceleration. Early layers maintain relatively constant velocities, but later layers exhibit increasing velocity, with the steepest acceleration in the last third of the model.
🎢 Analogy: Think of a roller coaster. The early layers are the slow climb up — steady, gradual changes. The later layers are the descent — rapid, dramatic transformations of the representation. The model "commits" to its final answer with increasing urgency.

📉 Finding 4: Mutual Information Drops Sharply

While correlations increase over layers, mutual information (MI) between consecutive layers shows a sharp decline in early layers. This is a seeming paradox: how can things become more correlated but share less information?
Resolution of the Paradox: Correlation captures only linear relationships. MI captures all dependencies (linear + nonlinear). The drop in MI with rising correlation means the model is discarding complex nonlinear relationships in favor of simpler, linearly-aligned features in later layers.

🌀 Finding 5: Units Trace Unstable Periodic Orbits

When plotting each unit's activation vs. its gradient (rate of change across layers), the resulting phase portraits reveal rotational dynamics — the units spiral outward like unstable periodic orbits. On average, each unit completes ~10.74 rotations through the 64 sublayers, compared to ~0 for shuffled controls.
What this means: Individual units don't just monotonically increase — they oscillate in a structured, spiral pattern. This is reminiscent of dynamics seen in biological neural circuits.

🗺️ Finding 6: Curved Trajectories in Low-Dimensional Space

Using both a Compressing Autoencoder (CAE) and PCA, the authors reduced the 4096-dimensional residual stream to just 2 dimensions. The resulting trajectories show a consistent curved path that straightens in later layers, with increasing distance between successive layer points.

Key observation: Early layers distribute variance across many dimensions; later layers require fewer principal components. The representation becomes increasingly low-dimensional as it progresses.

🧲 Finding 7: Self-Correcting Attractor-like Dynamics

When the authors "teleported" (perturbed) the residual stream to random points in the reduced space at various layers, they discovered self-correcting behavior: the trajectories tend to return toward the normal path, especially in middle layers (7, 15, 23). Perturbations at the very first or last layers are much harder to recover from.
🌊 Analogy: Like a river in a valley — if you teleport water to a nearby hillside, it flows back to the riverbed. But if you teleport it to a mountaintop (early layer) or over a waterfall (late layer), it may end up somewhere completely different.

🎯 Summary & Implications

This paper establishes a "neuroscience of AI" framework — applying dynamical systems tools from brain science to understand transformers. Here's what they found:
1. Continuity: Individual RS units maintain strong correlations across layers despite no privileged basis.
2. Densification: Activations systematically grow in magnitude and the stream accelerates through layers.
3. Information Compression: Sharp MI drop in early layers suggests fundamental transformation from complex to simple representations.
4. Rotational Dynamics: Units trace unstable periodic orbits (~10.74 rotations) in phase space.
5. Low-dimensional Flow: Despite 4096 dimensions, dynamics are remarkably low-dimensional with curved trajectories.
6. Self-correction: Mid-layer perturbations self-correct, suggesting stable computational channels.
🌟 The Big Picture: Transformers don't compute via isolated "grandmother cells" or sparse circuits alone. They implement distributed, flowing computations — more like a river system with currents, eddies, and self-correcting channels than a circuit board with discrete components.
