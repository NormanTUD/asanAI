Topology You Can See

Three interactive examples showing how the shape of a space changes everything — no formulas needed, just your eyes and mouse.

1. Pac-Man Lives on a Torus

In classic arcade games, going off the right edge brings you back on the left; going off the top brings you back at the bottom. This wrapping means the flat screen is actually a torus (donut shape)! Move the dot with arrow keys and watch it on both views simultaneously.

Flat "screen" view — ←→↑↓ to move
Same world on a torus — drag to rotate, scroll to zoom
Why this matters: Realizing the game world is a torus explains why there's no "edge" — the shortest path between two points might go through the wall! Speedrunners exploit this topology to find shorter routes that seem impossible on a flat map.

2. You Can't Comb a Hairy Ball (Wind on Earth)

The "Hairy Ball Theorem" says you can't comb a hairy sphere flat without creating at least one cowlick (a point where the hair swirls or vanishes). This is why Earth ALWAYS has at least one cyclone! Drag the globe to rotate it and see why calm everywhere is topologically impossible on a sphere.

Drag to rotate globe | Scroll to zoom | Click to add wind disturbance
Why this matters: This theorem guarantees that at any moment, somewhere on Earth the wind speed is exactly zero (the "eye" of a storm). It's not weather — it's pure topology! The same principle means every continuous vector field on a sphere has a zero. GPS satellites use this insight in their coverage algorithms.

3. Why Your Headphones Always Tangle

Ever pulled headphones from your pocket only to find them hopelessly knotted? This isn't bad luck — it's knot theory, a branch of topology. A loose string confined in a small space will almost certainly form knots when jostled. The longer the string, the more complex the knots. Press Tumble to simulate your pocket!

Why this matters: In 2007, physicists Raymer & Smith proved that a tumbled string almost inevitably forms knots, and the probability increases with length. This is why short charging cables tangle less! Topologically, a knot is a closed loop embedded in 3D space — and some knots (like the trefoil) can't be untangled into a simple loop no matter what you do. This same math helps biologists understand how DNA coils and uncoils inside your cells.
