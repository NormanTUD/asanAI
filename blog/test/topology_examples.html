Topology You Can See

Five interactive examples showing how the shape of a space changes everything — no formulas needed, just your eyes and arrow keys.

1. Pac-Man Lives on a Torus

In classic arcade games, going off the right edge brings you back on the left; going off the top brings you back at the bottom. This wrapping means the flat screen is actually a torus (donut shape)! Move the dot with arrow keys and watch it on both views simultaneously.

Flat "screen" view — ←→↑↓ to move
Same world on a torus — drag to rotate
Why this matters: Realizing the game world is a torus explains why there's no "edge" — the shortest path between two points might go through the wall! Speedrunners exploit this topology to find shorter routes that seem impossible on a flat map.

2. Möbius Strip: One-Sided Music Loop

A Möbius strip has only ONE side. An ant walking along it will visit "both sides" without ever crossing an edge. This is how some conveyor belts work (they wear evenly!) and how Bach's "Crab Canon" works — the melody plays forward AND backward simultaneously on the same strip.

Flat strip — watch the note travel — ←→ speed
Same strip twisted into Möbius — drag to rotate
Why this matters: Conveyor belts made as Möbius strips last twice as long because they use "both sides" (which are really one side). The topology gives you a practical engineering advantage — double the wear surface from the same material!

3. A Coffee Mug IS a Donut

Topologically, a coffee mug and a donut are the same — both have exactly one hole. You can continuously deform one into the other without cutting or gluing. Use the slider to watch the transformation happen smoothly.


Mug ← slide → Donut | ←→ also work
Why this matters: Topologists classify shapes by properties that survive stretching — like the number of holes. This is why topology is called "rubber sheet geometry." It helps in data analysis: if your dataset has a "hole" in it, that hole persists no matter how you stretch or compress the data, revealing hidden structure.

4. You Can't Comb a Hairy Ball (Wind on Earth)

The "Hairy Ball Theorem" says you can't comb a hairy sphere flat without creating at least one cowlick (a point where the hair swirls or vanishes). This is why Earth ALWAYS has at least one cyclone! Move the wind field and see why a calm everywhere is topologically impossible on a sphere.

←→ rotate globe | ↑↓ tilt | Click to add wind direction
Why this matters: This theorem guarantees that at any moment, somewhere on Earth the wind speed is exactly zero (the "eye" of a storm). It's not weather — it's pure topology! The same principle means every continuous vector field on a sphere has a zero. GPS satellites use this insight in their coverage algorithms.

5. Klein Bottle Maze: No Inside or Outside

A Klein bottle has no inside or outside — like a Möbius strip but closed. In this maze, what seems like a wall from one "side" is open from the "other" — but there's only one side! Navigate and discover paths that seem impossible in normal space.

Flat view (arrows identify) — ←→↑↓ to move
Klein bottle view — drag to rotate
Why this matters: Klein bottle topology appears in advanced robotics — when a robot arm can rotate fully AND flip, its configuration space is a Klein bottle. Understanding this prevents the robot from getting "stuck" in impossible configurations. It also appears in certain crystal structures in chemistry.
