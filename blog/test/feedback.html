🔁 Feedback Loops

A feedback loop is a cause-and-effect cycle where the system's output feeds back as input, creating self-reinforcing or self-correcting behavior. Once you see them, you'll find them everywhere.

Positive (Reinforcing) Feedback Loop

More leads to more. The output amplifies the input. Small changes snowball into massive effects. This is the engine behind exponential growth — and exponential collapse.

🏃

Skill & Confidence

Practice → improvement → confidence → more practice → mastery.

🏘️

Gentrification

Trendy shops open → area becomes desirable → prices rise → more trendy shops.

💡 Key Insight

Positive feedback loops are inherently unstable — they always accelerate toward explosion or collapse. Nothing in nature grows forever. Eventually, a negative feedback loop or a hard limit kicks in. When you spot a positive loop, always ask: "What will eventually stop this?"

Negative (Balancing) Feedback Loop

The system self-corrects toward a target. Deviations are counteracted. This is the engine behind stability, homeostasis, and regulation — thermostats, body temperature, supply & demand.

Thermostat

Room too cold → heater on → room warms → heater off. Maintains target temperature.

Blood Sugar

Sugar rises → insulin released → sugar drops → insulin stops. Your body auto-regulates.

💵

Supply & Demand

Price too high → fewer buyers → surplus → price drops. Markets self-correct.

🚿

Shower Temperature

Too hot → turn knob left → too cold → turn knob right → oscillate toward comfort.

😴

Sleep Pressure

Stay awake long → sleepiness builds → you sleep → sleepiness resets. Daily cycle.

🏛️

Checks & Balances

One branch overreaches → others push back → power re-balances. Designed stability.

💡 Key Insight

Negative feedback loops create stability, but they can also create stagnation. Every system that "resists change" is running a negative feedback loop. When you're stuck in a rut — a habit, a relationship pattern, an organization — look for the balancing loop that's keeping things the same. To change, you must disrupt the loop or change the target.

Combined: Growth Meets Limits

Real systems have both loops running simultaneously. A positive loop drives growth, but a negative loop enforces a carrying capacity. The result: the famous S-curve — slow start, explosive middle, plateau at the end.

💡 The Meta-Insight

Almost every real-world phenomenon is a combination of feedback loops competing with each other. The question is never "is there a feedback loop?" — there always is. The question is: "Which loop is dominant right now, and when will the other one take over?" That's where the leverage is. That's where you can predict what happens next.
