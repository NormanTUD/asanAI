Signal Basics

What is a signal? A signal is any quantity that varies over time (or space). The simplest signal is a sinusoid, described by amplitude, frequency, and phase. All complex signals can be built from these simple building blocks.

Signal Addition

Signals can be added together. Below you see two sinusoids and their sum — this is the foundation of Fourier analysis: any signal is a sum of sinusoids.

Fourier Series Approximation

Key Insight: Any periodic signal can be decomposed into a sum of sines and cosines (harmonics). The Fourier series lets us approximate complex waveforms by adding up simple sinusoids. Move the slider to add more harmonics and watch the approximation improve!

Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)

The DFT takes a finite sequence of samples and computes the frequency content. It's the practical tool used in all digital signal processing. The equation correlates the signal with complex exponentials at each frequency bin.
Euler's Formula: The DFT uses complex exponentials. Each frequency bin k measures how much the signal "correlates" with a complex sinusoid at that frequency. The magnitude tells you the amplitude, the phase tells you the time shift.

Signal Filtering

Filtering means selectively removing or keeping certain frequency components. A low-pass filter keeps low frequencies, a high-pass keeps high frequencies, and a band-pass keeps a specific range.

Sampling & Aliasing (Nyquist Theorem)

Nyquist-Shannon Theorem: To perfectly reconstruct a signal, you must sample at least twice the highest frequency present. If you sample too slowly, high frequencies "fold" into lower ones — this is aliasing.
Status: Sampling rate is sufficient (no aliasing).

Wavelet Analysis

Wavelets vs Fourier: Fourier analysis tells you WHAT frequencies are present but not WHEN they occur. Wavelets give you time-frequency localization — you can see which frequencies are active at which times. This is crucial for non-stationary signals.
The scalogram (bottom) shows signal energy across time and scale (inversely related to frequency). Bright areas indicate where a particular frequency is active.

Holographic Data Storage

Holography stores information as interference patterns. When two coherent waves (reference and object beams) interfere, they create a pattern that encodes both amplitude AND phase information. This is fundamentally a signal processing concept — the interference pattern is the Fourier transform of the stored data!
Left: The interference pattern (hologram) stored in the medium. Right: Reconstruction when illuminated with the reference beam — the original object information is recovered via diffraction (which is essentially a Fourier transform performed by light!).
