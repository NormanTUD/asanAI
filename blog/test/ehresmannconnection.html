The Ehresmann Connection

An interactive visual guide to understanding connections on fibre bundles — the geometric idea of splitting tangent spaces into horizontal and vertical directions.

What is a Fibre Bundle?

A fibre bundle π : E → M is a space E (the total space) that locally looks like a product M × F, where M is the base manifold and F is the fibre. The map π projects each point in E down to a point in M.

The Vertical Subspace

At any point p ∈ E, the vertical subspace VpE = ker(dπp) consists of tangent vectors that point along the fibre — i.e., vectors that get killed by the projection's derivative. This subspace is canonical: it requires no extra choice.

Move the point up and down the fibre to see the vertical tangent vector.

The Ehresmann Connection = Choosing a Horizontal Subspace

An Ehresmann connection is a smooth choice of a horizontal subspace HpE at every point p ∈ E such that TpE = VpE ⊕ HpE.

This is not canonical — there are many possible choices! Use the angle slider to rotate the horizontal subspace and see different connections.

Parallel Transport

Once a connection (horizontal distribution) is chosen, we can parallel-transport a point in the fibre along a curve in the base. At each instant, we move in the horizontal direction — the unique lift of the base velocity into HpE.

Click "Transport!" to watch a point get carried along the base curve. Change the connection angle to see how different connections yield different transport results.

Curvature = Failure of Horizontal Distribution to be Integrable

The curvature of an Ehresmann connection measures whether the horizontal distribution H is integrable (i.e., whether horizontal lifts of closed loops return to the same fibre point). If the curvature is zero, parallel transport around any closed loop is trivial. If it is nonzero, you get holonomy.

Drag the curvature slider to see how a round-trip on the base produces a shift (holonomy) in the fibre.
