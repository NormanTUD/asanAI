A Word Is Not a Point.
It's a Helix.

In a Transformer's embedding space, a token's raw embedding is a single fixed point. But the moment you add sinusoidal positional encoding, that point gets swept along a high-dimensional helical curve. Every position maps the same word to a different spot on that helix. The word becomes a spiral through space.

Why a Helix?

The positional encoding formula assigns each position a vector built from sine and cosine pairs at different frequencies:

PE(pos, 2i) = sin(pos / 10000^(2i/d)) PE(pos, 2i+1) = cos(pos / 10000^(2i/d))

Each dimension pair (2i, 2i+1) traces a circle as pos increases — because (sin θ, cos θ) is a point on the unit circle. But different pairs rotate at different speeds:

  • Dims 0, 1 (highest frequency): rotate fast — the circular cross-section
  • Dims 2, 3 (lower frequency): rotate slowly — the axial drift
  • Dims 4, 5, 6, 7…: even slower, adding drift in higher dimensions

The result is a corkscrew: the fast pair draws circles, the slow pairs stretch those circles along additional axes. In 3D this is a classic helix. In the full d-dimensional space, it's a curve on a high-dimensional torus (a product of circles) [[11]].

Why not a sphere? A sphere would mean positions scatter uniformly in all directions. But positions are ordered. Position 5 sits between 4 and 6 on the curve. The helix preserves sequential ordering geometrically: adjacent positions are adjacent points on the curve [[11]].

The FFN never sees the raw embedding or the positional encoding separately. It receives only the sum — a single vector that is a point on the word's helix, after it has also been mixed by self-attention [[11]].

The FFN must learn to disentangle:

  • "Which helix am I on?" → word identity
  • "Where on the helix am I?" → position in the sequence

Why This Geometry Matters

1. Adjacency is geometric

Position 42 and 43 are neighbours on the helix. The model doesn't memorise "42 is next to 43" — it's encoded in the curve's continuity [[11]].

2. Relative distance = fixed rotation

"3 positions apart" always corresponds to the same rotation matrix M₃, whether at position 2 or 2000. This group structure lets the model generalise to unseen sequence lengths [[11]].

3. The helix never self-intersects

The incommensurate frequencies ensure the curve never returns to the same point. Every position gets a truly unique encoding — the same principle that makes irrational rotations on a torus produce dense, non-repeating orbits [[11]].

4. Why not tanh or sigmoid?

Functions like tanh(pos) saturate at ±1 and lack the rotation property: there is no matrix M_k such that tanh(pos + k) = M_k · tanh(pos). Without that algebraic structure, the model cannot generalise relative distances [[11]].
