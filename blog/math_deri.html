1. The Intuition — Slopes & Tangent Lines

The derivative of a function at a point tells you the instantaneous rate of change — the slope of the tangent line at that point. Imagine zooming in on a curve until it looks like a straight line. The slope of that line is the derivative.

2. The Limit Definition

The formal definition of the derivative is:

f'(x) = limh→0 [ f(x + h) − f(x) ] / h

This is the slope of the secant line between (x, f(x)) and (x+h, f(x+h)) as h shrinks to zero. Watch the secant line approach the tangent line as you decrease h:

💡 Key insight: As h → 0, the yellow secant line converges to the pink tangent line. The secant slope approaches the true derivative.

4. Constant & Constant Multiple Rules

d/dx [ c ] = 0     (constant rule)
d/dx [ c · f(x) ] = c · f'(x)     (constant multiple rule)

5. Sum & Difference Rule

d/dx [ f(x) ± g(x) ] = f'(x) ± g'(x)

The derivative of a sum (or difference) is the sum (or difference) of the derivatives. Below we visualize f(x) = x³ and g(x) = x², their sum, and the derivative of the sum.

6. Product Rule

d/dx [ f(x) · g(x) ] = f'(x)·g(x) + f(x)·g'(x)

Let f(x) = x and g(x) = sin(x). Then the product is x·sin(x) and its derivative is sin(x) + x·cos(x).

💡 Mnemonic: "First times derivative of second, plus second times derivative of first" — or simply f'g + fg'.

7. Quotient Rule

d/dx [ f(x)/g(x) ] = [ f'(x)·g(x) − f(x)·g'(x) ] / [ g(x) ]²

Example: f(x) = sin(x), g(x) = x. So we differentiate sin(x)/x (the sinc-like function).

💡 Mnemonic: "Low d-high minus high d-low, over the square of what's below."

8. Chain Rule

d/dx [ f(g(x)) ] = f'(g(x)) · g'(x)

The chain rule handles compositions. Example: f(x) = sin(x²). Here the outer function is sin(u) and the inner function is u = x². The derivative is cos(x²) · 2x.

Another Example: Nested Chain Rule

f(x) = esin(x) → f'(x) = esin(x) · cos(x)

💡 Think of it as peeling layers: Differentiate the outer function, leave the inner alone, then multiply by the derivative of the inner function. Repeat for deeper nesting.

10. Exponential & Logarithmic Derivatives

d/dx [ eˣ ] = eˣ
d/dx [ aˣ ] = aˣ · ln(a)
d/dx [ ln(x) ] = 1/x
d/dx [ loga(x) ] = 1 / (x · ln(a))

11. Higher-Order Derivatives

You can differentiate a derivative to get the second derivative f''(x), then the third derivative f'''(x), and so on.

  • f'(x) — velocity (rate of change)
  • f''(x) — acceleration (rate of change of rate of change)
  • f'''(x) — jerk

Below: f(x) = sin(x) and its first four derivatives.

💡 Notice how the 4th derivative of sin(x) is sin(x) again — the cycle repeats every 4 differentiations! The purple dotted line sits exactly on top of the blue line.

12. Applications — Maxima, Minima & Inflection Points

Derivatives are the key tool for analyzing the shape of a function:

  • f'(x) = 0 → critical points (potential maxima or minima)
  • f''(x) > 0 at a critical point → local minimum (concave up)
  • f''(x) < 0 at a critical point → local maximum (concave down)
  • f''(x) = 0 and sign change → inflection point

Example: f(x) = x³ − 3x + 1. We have f'(x) = 3x² − 3 and f''(x) = 6x.

Critical points at x = ±1. f''(−1) = −6 < 0 → local max. f''(1) = 6 > 0 → local min. Inflection at x = 0.
