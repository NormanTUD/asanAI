9. KV-Cache: Why Inference Is Cheaper Than You Think
You explain the autoregressive loop but don't mention the KV-cache, which is the key engineering insight that makes inference practical. During generation, the Keys and Values for all previous tokens don't change — only the new token's Q, K, V need to be computed. So the model caches all previous K and V tensors, turning each generation step from O(n2)O(n2) to O(n)O(n). This is why LLMs can generate long sequences at reasonable speed, and why context window size is primarily a memory constraint, not a compute constraint 


6. Why Cross-Entropy Loss Specifically?
You explain MSE and Cross-Entropy as loss functions, but the deeper aha-moment is why cross-entropy is the natural choice for classification. It comes from information theory: minimizing cross-entropy is equivalent to maximizing the likelihood of the training data under the model's predicted distribution. And the gradient of cross-entropy with softmax is the beautifully simple (ppred−ptrue)(ppred​−ptrue​) — the error signal is just "how wrong were you?" This is why it pairs so perfectly with softmax

7. The Jacobian and Why Backpropagation is a Matrix Chain
Your autodiff section is thorough, but you could add the insight that backpropagation through a network is really just multiplying a chain of Jacobian matrices. Each layer's local derivative is a Jacobian, and the chain rule is matrix multiplication. The vanishing gradient problem is then immediately obvious: if every Jacobian has singular values < 1, the product shrinks exponentially. ResNets fix this because their Jacobian is I+JFI+JF​ — the identity matrix guarantees singular values ≥ 1

12. Token Prediction and the Phenomenology of Time
Here's a more speculative one. LLMs process text autoregressively — each token is generated conditioned on all previous tokens, creating an asymmetric temporal arrow. The model has a "past" (the context window) but no "future" until it generates it.This mirrors Husserl's phenomenology of time-consciousness: the present moment is always constituted by retention (memory of what just happened) and protention (anticipation of what comes next).
