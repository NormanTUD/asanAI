9. The "Reversal Curse" Is a Consequence of the Causal Mask, Not a Failure of Understanding
You discuss the Reversal Curse in your hallucinations section as a reasoning failure, but there's a precise technical explanation. The model is trained autoregressively: given "A is B," it learns P(B∣A is)P(B∣A is). The causal mask means that when processing "A is B," the token "B" can attend to "A" and "is," but "A" cannot attend to "B."When asked "Who is B?", the model must compute P(A∣B is)P(A∣B is). But during training, the token "A" was never in a position where it needed to be predicted after "B" — the information flowed in the wrong direction. The model would need to have seen "B is A" in the training data to learn this reverse mapping.The aha-moment: the Reversal Curse is not a failure of "understanding" — it's a direct consequence of the asymmetric information flow imposed by the causal mask. The model literally never had the gradient signal to learn the reverse direction. This is a fundamental limitation of autoregressive training: it learns P(B∣A)P(B∣A) but not P(A∣B)P(A∣B) unless both directions appear in the data. Bidirectional models like BERT don't suffer from this because every token attends to every other token. The Reversal Curse reveals that autoregressive models learn conditional distributions, not joint distributions, and these are not interchangeable without Bayes' theorem — which the model has no mechanism to apply.
